Thoughts on my Thirty Eighth Year

Date:2023-February-12

Today is my birthday, so I'm going to go ahead and take one of my "steadholders" for the year. I had hoped to get a new take on the Hail Idun prayer in Germanish meter, but my first few drafts aren't doing it for me, and since it will be the beating heart of the Heathen Rosary, I want to get it right. Instead, a few things I've been thinking about:

  • 38 truly feels a lot closer to 40 than 37, but even that was "not my 'mid-thirties' anymore." I've always sought to not get too hung up on youth and my age, and to know and accept my own looming death, but it didn't fully hit me until 2018 when my mother passed away and my first daughter was born within a couple of months of each other. That was one hell of a shot of "you're only one step in the ongoing march of life" all at once.
  • My older daughter is doing her bit to make me feel the full weight of growing older. Yesterday, she asked me "Daddy, are you an old man?" and I said "no, not yet, but I'm on my way there." So, she said "will you be an old man tomorrow?" and again "no, not just yet." "Well, when will you be an old man?" "Not for a while, honey."
  • More earnestly, my deepening spiritual work has been a big help with acknowledging and dealing with my feelings about getting older. I truly don't know where I'd be had the last few years gone otherwise. These days, my only regret is that I didn't find this path sooner, but I keep telling myself it's not a race, there's no prize for "doing more," it's not about what's at the end of the path, it's all about walking it.

With that said, I put forth my blessings to you and hope the coming year treats you wonderfully.

Thoughts on The Way of the Rose

Date: 2023-February-05

The very first book that I dug into after my year with dead writers was The Way of the Rose: The Radical Path of the Divine Feminine Hidden in the Rosary by Clark Strand and Perdita Finn. Someone had spoken well of it on a Magic Monday over on JMG's dreamwidth journal, and it sounded like it would be helpful to me in my Heathen Rosary work. Overall, I found the book helpful and thought-stirring, but below, I share my thoughts in more depth.

In a nutshell, the book makes the case that the Rosary is a very helpful tool for spiritual growth and healing that need not be only for Catholics, or even Christians. The book is written with one chapter for every bead on a stock Rosary, and the chapters are grouped by the old mysteries (so no "Luminous mysteries"). Through these chapters, the writers put forth that the Rosary represents a very, very old strand of Goddess worship squelched by the church that nevertheless found its way into folks' daily practice. They give some slightly changed ways of saying the prayers to make them a bit more welcoming for those who are not Christians. They also give some thoughts on how to pray, such as getting together in "Rosary Groups". The bulk of the book speaks of the writers' own spiritual paths with the Rosary: coming to it through "coincidences" and visions, deepening connection with it, and some "whoah, this [stuff] works" moments. Along the way, they talk about how the Rosary came to be and what has happened with it, of the Church, of religion more broadly, and their own backgrounds in spiritual seeking. If any of this sounds like it might be interesting, read on!

I said at the start that my goal in reading this book was to deepen my understanding of the Rosary as a step on the way to crafting my "Heathen Rosary", and weirdly enough, this book might as well have been written for that very end. Okay, okay, not wholly that, but their way of talking about the Rosary was remarkably helpful to me in a few ways. First, the weight they put on the Rosary as a way of getting a hold of the "Divine Feminine", but matched with calls to the "Divine Masculine", lined up with one of my gut-feelings about what makes the Rosary work so well. Next, my way of working has been to "back-craft" the Rosary, to work on understanding what makes it tick beyond "it's contemplation while saying the same prayers over and over with beads to keep track". As such, their use of a almost the stock Rosary, but with a very unalike theological understanding and goal, meant that a lot of their insights were worthwhile to me. Lastly, the book highlights the slow, steady insight and wisdom that comes from ongoing work, even if you don't know why/how/whether it's working, which matches how spiritual practice has worked out for me so far.

To take these as they were named, the first thing was weighty enough to show up in the under-name (subtitle) of the book: the Rosary is a way of getting in touch with the "Divine Feminine." While this was one of the main ways the book was helpful to me, it also touches on one of the book's biggest drawbacks for me, which is that the writers seem to believe something like "all Goddesses are one Goddess". As such, they see worship of mother Goddesses in the paleolithic caves of France, the Eleusinian Mysteries, and praying the Rosary all as unalike ways of giving prayer and worship to the same holy Being. This is what is sometimes called soft polytheism, which I think is likely an overly shallow take on what's going on in the spiritual world. This hurdle is fairly readily dealt with, but it's also there throughout the whole book. Even so, I think they rightly mark out some threads of what worship for feminine beings is like and bring those threads out clearly in the Rosary and what it has to teach. What kind of things do I mean? Things like kindness, forgiveness, open-heartedness, and wisdom. The book doesn't highlight as much the matching (not to say equal weight) between the masculine and feminine held in the Rosary. Each decade of ten "Hail Marys" opens with an "Our Father" and closes with a "Glory Be" (which calls upon the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost) - so you can see that Mary (the feminine) is very much at the heart of the practice, but with a weighty masculine match. I think it likely that this is a weighty bit of what makes the Rosary so mighty spiritually, and I want to have the same overall take for my Heathen Rosary.

Next, this book is indeed helpful for my "back-crafting" work, since it takes the Rosary earnestly, but is not bound by Catholic theology or doctrine. This opens the writers up to insights about how it works and what it's doing that a Catholic might either not heed or else make match up with the overall shape of their set of beliefs. For one ready showdeal, the set of prayers for the Rosary given in the book leaves out the Apostle's Creed at the beginning, because that is a downright affirmation that "yes, I believe the things Christianity has to teach" and puts the whole prayer in that framework. A Catholic likely deems that a needful bit, where Strand and Finn see it as a "lock" put on the gate to keep out those the Church wouldn't want saying "its" prayer. Likewise, in talking through the mysteries to be contemplated while praying the Rosary, a Catholic like de Montford will strongly ground them in the wider framework of Catholic theology, with a lot of steering on what a right understanding of each mystery might be. This work, on the other hand, looks at the mysteries as one set of tales showing wider spiritual truths. You don't have to believe that 2,000 years ago a Rabbi's dead body woke up and walked out of a cave to find loveliness, meaning, and Truth of a less straightforward kind in the tale of the rebirth. I'm most of all thankful that the writers spelled out how both the mysteries and the words of the "Hail Mary" go from life to suffering to death to rebirth. I may not want my Heathen Rosary to go through the same steps, but I do want the same kind of deep, framing resonances between the prayers themselves and the overall practice. To bring in an overly-leaned-upon forlikening (metaphor): this book has worked like a Rosetta Stone for the Rosary for me - rather than needing to translate from Catholic to Heathen with nothing in between, I can see what translating the Rosary into other spiritual "tongues" looks like, which is helpful.

Thirdly, the book spoke to a way of coming at spiritual growth that I seem to need to work on - steadfast, bit-by-bit unfolding of wisdom and insight through ongoing work. To fully share what I mean, I might have to lay out a bit about myself. Growing up, I was always a "smart kid" - I understood things quickly, soaked up new knowledge, and otherwise was ready to do things with my mind. I say this not to brag, as you'll see, but to highlight that I was (and largely still am) "brainy", maybe to the harm of other ways of knowing and understanding the world. This was made painfully clear to me in the aftermath of my mother passing away from cancer. Mind-wise, I had settled myself with what had happened, what it meant to me, how I should think and feel about it, and I thought I was alright. But I was not alright. My wife at last got me to go talk to a therapist, and through that and some of my own looking back, I had the insight that I was so wont to being able to think my way through things, to use my mind to handle stuff in my life, that I thought my feelings would play by the same rules, to answer to the same ways of doing things. In the same way that as a kid in math class, after doing a few problems I "got" how to bring to bear the principle and found further going over boring and unneeded, I thought that reading a few books, thinking about what happened in a few other folks' lives meant that I "got" grief and could go about my business with that little hardship cleanly worked out. As will no doubt be blindingly clear to you, unless you share the same quirk of which I've been telling, this is not at all how grief or any other weighty feeling works. You have to, you know, truly feel it, and that takes time and cannot be bossed around by the words in your head. What does work is daily, plodding work, over and over again, whether you're "feeling it" this time or not. Whether prayer, meditation, journaling, or whatever other spiritual practice, every time I have buckled down and done it no matter what, that is where I have been gifted with worthwhile spiritual experiences. The book had a lovely quote on coming at the Rosary in this way:

Rosary wisdom tends to grow slowly, building gradually over the course of a lifetime. The rosary isn't a degree program. There are no classes or courses. There's no diploma at the end. There isn't any end. We never stop accumulating the wisdom of the rosary. The beads turn, the seasons pass. The experiences of a lifetime settle within us like fallen leaves and flowers, becoming the top-soil that nourishes and informs our lives.

Overall, I found this book insightful and helpful, and I'm very glad I read it. That being said, it won't grab everyone alike. For starters, if the Rosary holds no spiritual or aesthetic appeal for you, I doubt that this book will change that. For another, Strand and Clark are coming at this from an openly "feminist" outlook, which brings along with it ways of framing and understanding things that you may or may not find helpful. One showdeal is their strong dislike and wariness of hierarchy, most of all those mostly made up of men, as seen when they speak rather harshly of "religion" many times in the book. By "religion" they seem to mean any group with any hierarchy given to spiritual ends. Another showdeal is that in a few steads they treat masculine ways of understanding or dealing with the world as childish or mistaken, things that need to be outgrown or put aside, rather than matched with feminine ways for a healthy whole. For both of these showdeals, they share things they have lived through that make it rather understandable how they would come to this way of looking at the world, including some truly wretched dealings with so-called spiritual teachers, but I still think they've "learned too much" from those happenings. Lastly, they speak in rather apocalyptic words about the way the world is going - it's not so much that I doubt things things are going to go poorly, but rather that I don't foresee the sudden and utter outcomes held in apocalyptic thinking about what may come. I think a slow, ragged descent is a far more likely forecast.

So, if you'd like to learn more about the Rosary from a truly not-Christian way of looking at the world, or maybe work at understanding some of what folks have found in praying the Rosary, this book's a great way to do that.

Mark of the Wells

Date: 2023-January-29

In my ongoing work on a Heathen Rosary, I hit upon the thought that I ought to have something much like the Christian "Sign of the Cross" - a quick prayer and bodily token of self-blessing and warding. As with much of this undertaking, I started from the thought that Christian, most of all older kinds of Christian, tradition has much that works well, and maybe it doesn't only work for Christian beliefs. The things that drew me to the Sign of the Cross were 1) it is rather short, 2) in that short length, it calls upon a whole, balanced holiness, and 3) it is linked with energy centers in the subtle body. These hallmarks let the Sign of the Cross be something that Christians use both day-to-day (before and after longer prayers, when coming to the altar, and so forth) and also "as needed", such as coming across something dreadful or grasping that a statement ought to have been a prayer.

So, I set about coming up with such a short prayer-with-gesture that works for my own beliefs and worship. What I came up with largely came from intuition, meditation, prayer, and divination. I haven't tested it out much yet, so if anyone else finds this helpful, I'd love to hear from you. Let me break down how I matched up the bits I laid out above:

  1. To make it short, I settled on Galdralag meter. I also tried out some others, such as four lines of Anglo-Saxon Long Lines, but those felt a bit long for what I was looking for.
  2. To get a whole, balanced likeness of the spiritual world, I began with the thought of the World Tree, Yggdrasil. As I worked on this and meditated, though, it came to me to instead focus mainly on the three wells from which the World Tree's roots drink - in Old Norse, Hvergelmir, Mimisbrunnr, and Urdabrunnr (more on these below when I talk about the words I picked).
  3. For the energy centers, I drew on those marked out in John Michael Greer's Druidic magical systems, such as that laid out in his Druid Magic Handbook. These are the "womb center" about an inch below the belly button, the "third eye" between the eyebrows, and the heart center (unsurprisingly, at the heart).

Again, I got to much of this in a way I can't wholly spell out in a way I can back up, but it feels pretty strong to me nonetheless.

So, what of the prayer itself? As I said, I used Galdralag, and here's what I came up with:

From seething hwer1,// Woden's drink,// and Wyrd's holy well,// Mighty tree unmatched.

1 An Old English word meaning "boiler, cauldron"

A few thoughts on the words I picked. First, "hwer" was a bit of an odd one, but I couldn't land on a translation of Hvergelmir I liked much. Hwer is the direct cognate with Old Norse hver, a word for a pot or a kettle. The gelmir bit is cognate with "yelling", but "Yelling Pot" just doesn't have much of a ring to it in English, to say nothing of making the alliteration work. The sense of Hvergelmir seems to be something like churning rapids, or a steaming, boiling hot spring. "Woden's Drink" is a pretty straightforward kenning for Meomer's (Mimir's) Well, the well of wisdom that Woden gave his eye to drink from. Wyrd's holy well is, of course, the Old Norse Urdhabrunnr, Wyrd being a direct cognate for Urdh. The "mighty tree unmatched" is, also of course, the World Tree, Yggdrasil.

So, here's how I've been doing it:

  1. Take 2 fingers and put them about an inch below your belly button and say "From seething hwer,"
  2. Move your fingers to the spot between your eyebrows and say "Woden's drink,"
  3. Move your fingers to your heart and say "and Wyrd's holy well, Mighty tree unmatched."

This ought to work for Heathen's who'd like an all-around self-blessing/warding to say before and/or after prayer, when running into wicked things, and so forth. I'll start using it, and I'll let you know what I find out.

List of Books on Germanish Belief with Thoughts

Date: 2023-January-22

I very much like when a writer shares not only the books that they read, but also what they think of them - an "annotated bibliography". I may not have a book of my own (yet), but I do have a big ol' list of books I have read or want to read on Germanish belief, and I thought I'd share it. As I get to the books I haven't read on this list, I might come back and add my thoughts on them. If you know of any books you think ought to be on the list, kindly let me know! For now, I'll share my thoughts either from reading the book, or what I know about it that makes me want to read it.

Also, it ought to go without saying, but given the times we live in, I'll say it anyhow: my putting a book on the list below does not mean I agree with it in whole or in part - it only means that I think it might have some insights on understanding the field it's about.

Wellsprings of the Lore

These are the elder works that everything else builds upon. Mostly written down by Christians, and mostly written sometime after folks worshiped the Germanish Gods and Goddesses, we are nevertheless lucky to have far more to work with than the beliefs of many other lands. These works show us a tantalizing glimpse of the wider world of Gods, Goddesses, elves, dwarves, and other wights that the folks of Germanish lands lived in before the coming of Christianity.

The Prose Edda

By: Snorri Sturlusson
Have I Read It?: Yes, more than once, from many translators
Thoughts: This is the book to read on Old Norse mythology. If you read nothing else, read this one. Helpfully put-together and makes good sense, maybe too much sometimes, but most of the "summaries of Old Norse Mythology" below are mostly restatements of this. More than one good translation out there, but I like the Jesse Byock one or Jackson Crawford's.

The Poetic Edda

By: Many writers
Have I Read It?: Yes
Thoughts: In many ways, this is even more grounded than The Prose Edda, but it's harder to follow if you haven't already read Sturlusson's learned summary. Jackson Crawford's translation is helpfully literal, Lee Hollander's has a delightful Germanish taste thanks to the words he uses, and Maria Kvilhaug's take in her book Seed of Yggdrasil (see below) gives the meaning of the proper names, which is a big help to understanding.

The Sagas of the Icelanders

By: Many writers
Have I Read It?: Some of it
Thoughts: I have a lovely collection of all of the sagas in translation, available here. These works give a lot of hints about what pre-Christian belief and worship looked like, but it takes some amount of combing to make the most of them.

Germania

By: Tacitus
Have I Read It?: Bits
Thoughts: This is maybe our oldest written take on what the Germanish folks of the time believed and did for worship. It has the great strength of not being written by Christians, which gives a very different look at things.

Gesta Danorum

By: Saxo Grammaticus
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: This re-tells many of the stories found in the Prose Edda and tells a few others not known elsewhere, but through the "euhemerization" lens. Basically, Saxo believes that the Heathen lore was a garbled way of talking about normal humans that lived in the world. So, he needs to be taken with lots of salt, but he shares a lot that can't be found elsewhere.

Heimskringla

By: Snorri Sturlusson
Have I Read It?: No]] Thoughts: This is a history of the kings of Norway and Sweden written by the same heavy hitter who gave us the Prose Edda. Though written as a "history," the older bits likely draw on beliefs from before Christianity. For translations, Lee Hollander's is good, but there are very many.

Beowulf

By: Unknown, translated by Howell D. Chickering
Have I Read It?: Yes
Thoughts: The one, best-known poem from Old English times, it was written down by a Christian monk, but it holds many beliefs from older times. This translation comes in dual-language edition that shows the Old English text, and on the facing page.

The Legend of Sigurd and Gudrun

By: Unknown, translated by J.R.R. Tolkien
Have I Read It?: Ye
Thoughts: Not only a translation, this tells a coherent version of the story of Sigurd and Gudrun, which are told in multiple places in multiple languages. Tolkien put it together following the rules of Old Norse/Old English poetic meter in modern English, which makes for a lovely read

Other Old English Works

There's a fairly large body of work in Old English, much of it poetry, but all written down by Christians. Even so, there are hints and clues to older belief salted throughout, and if nothing else, you can get an understanding of the folkways that kept going after switching over to Christianity that might help us understand what folks were like before Christianity..

The Complete Old English Poems

By: Many Writers, Translated by Craig Williamson
Have I Read It?: Bits
Thoughts: Lots of stuff to work with, but it is a big body of lore, which may not be of as much help as things like the Prose Edda, as much of it is very Christian. Still, the good news is there is a lot of it, and that means lots of small hints and glimpses scattered throughout.

The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle

By: Many Writers, Translated by Michael Swanton]] Have I Read It?: Yes
Thoughts: Written down to keep track of what happened during which King's rule, this is another thing written down by Christians, but with bits to help out those who want to know more about beliefs before the Christians.

Learned Works (Academic Works)

These books are written by men and women building upon earlier writings by the learned and working hard to back up what they say with evidence. Most of the books below sit right on the line between those written for other learned men and women and those written for everyday folks.

This may well end up the longest bit of this list, so I've broken it up by fields. Many of these books could rightly be put in more than one of these fields, but I've put them where they seem most fitting to me.

Works on Gods, Goddesses, Worship, and Lore (Religion, Mythology, and Folklore)

These works are all about the Gods, Goddesses, other Beings without bodies, and their worship and lore. These works take the wellsprings above and try to put them together with things dug up from the past, looking at the ways of worship of other Indo-European folks, and so forth.

Myths of the Pagan North

By: Christopher Abram
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: One of the many overviews of Old Norse lore. I have to confess that I have many of these, but have not much read them, as I feel like I already know most of what they hold. That being said, besides their main use (helping folks new to the lore get to know it), they are also helpful to more knowledgeable folks since they bring together in one stead what we know of the tales, beliefs, and worship about each God, the evidence for which might be spread across many works of elder times.

The Poetic Edda (Commentary)

By: Paul Acker and Carolyne Larrington
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: A gathering of the thoughts of learned folks on the poems of the Poetic Edda. I haven't dug in yet, but an Ecosophia reader spoke well of it. I gather that Larrington brings an at least somewhat "feminist" lens to looking at Germanish lore, which is rather unlike most of the other folks I read, and so ought to give some new ways of understanding things.

The Well and the Tree

By: Paul Bauschatz
Have I Read It?: Some
Thoughts: I'm reading this at the time of putting this list together, and I'm finding it rather interesting. It seems that Bauschatz mostly uses linguistic evidence to argue that the speakers of Germanish tongues thought about time in a rather unalike way to how we do now or how Roman and Christian writers did, and that we misunderstand what myths meant when we put today's way of looking at time on to them. Very hard to find, but try your nearest university bookhoard.

The Lost Gods of England

By: Brian Branston
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Unlike many of the books on this list, this one looks more to old things rather than old words - it goes over the archaeological evidence for the Gods of pre-Christian England. Some online reviews say that Branston is overly ready to see what he wants to see, but even if so, I would often rather have a writer willing to do that than one who is so careful with finding meaning that you are left with nothing useful for someone seeking to shape their own worship.

Gods and Myths of Northern Europe

By: Hilda R. Ellis Davidson
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Once again, an overview, but this one by a writer who is one of the best known in the field. Davidson was one of the first folks coming from the field of speechlore (philology) to look also at what can be known from digging up old things (archaeology).

The Lost Beliefs of Northern Europe

By: Hilda R. Ellis Davidson
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: While Gods and Myths is more about the tales of the Gods, this book seems to look more at what we know about how they were worshipped.

Myths and Symbols in Pagan Europe

By: Hilda R. Ellis Davidson
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: I am very much looking forward to reading this one, as it looks at both Germanish and Celtic lore and talks through some of the likenesses between them and how those might have come about. This one hits right in the middle of the Venn diagram overlap of my interest in Germanish Lore and my work with Celtic lore through Revival Druidry.

The Road to Hel

By: Hilda R. Ellis Davidson
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Maybe the most thoroughgoing breakdown of what the Germanish folks of elder times seemed to have believed about death, dying, and the afterlife. Pretty much every book below written by today's believers for worship draws heavily on this.

Lady with a Mead Cup

By: Michael J. Enright
Have I Read It?: Some
Thoughts: I started reading this and had to put it down for my year reading only books by the dead, but I think it will be very worthwhile to get back to. Enright lays out the evidence that Celtic and Germanish warbands of the volkerwanderung times had a role for a woman as seeress and initiator, and that you can see hints of this throughout the later lore, even as late as Beowulf. This seems rather weighty for understanding Germanish Goddesses, maybe Freyja most of all.

Gods and Worshipers in the Viking and Germanic World

By: Thor Ewing
Have I Read It?: Yes
Thoughts: It's been a while since I read this, but I remember finding it very interesting. The name is maybe overly-broad for what the book is truly about, which is making the case that berserkers and valkyries were real-life groups of worshippers of Odin. I remember thinking that maybe it went a bit too far into "explaining" mythical elements as real-life things done by worshippers, but I think the book is largely right.

The Interpretation of Fairy Tales

By: Marie-Louise von Franz
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: A student of Carl Jung's, von Franz wrote rather a lot books. I've pulled out the two that I am most looking forward to reading and that seem to have the most bearing on Germanish belief. This one gives an overall take on understanding fairy tales as expressions of Jung's archetypes, which I have found a very helpful way of getting more meaning out of such tales.

Shadow and Evil in Fairy Tales

By: Marie-Louise von Franz
Have I Read It?: Bits
Thoughts: Much like the above, but focused on the "Shadow" archetype, and how that shows itself in evil beings in fairy tales.

Aspects of Anglo-Saxon Magic

By: Bill Griffiths
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: This seems to be a gathering of the evidence for how the Anglo-Saxons did magic - written charms, things dug up, bits of folk belief that seem to have old roots, and so forth.

Teutonic Mythology

By: Jacob Grimm (translator Jason Stallybrass)
Have I Read It?: Bits
Thoughts: You can say with much fairness that this monster work is the granddaddy of the whole field. Most folks only know about Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm for the fairy tales they gathered, and don't know that they were mostly gathering those tales to find hints about the Gods, lore, and beliefs of elder Germanish folks. While some of what Grimm puts forward has been deemed wrong by later writers, much of it holds up, and if nothing else, his writing is a nearly unbelievable wealth of clues to Germanish folkways, beliefs, lore, and worship beyond the obvious ones from the Eddas and Sagas.

Grimm's Fairy Tales

By: Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Though having less bearing on the beliefs and worship of the Germanish folks of elder times than Jacob's great work, there are nonetheless lots of clues scattered throughout. Also, these tales are fun to read!

The Culture of the Teutons

By: Vilhelm Gronbech
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Many of the works for today's worshipers below mark out this book as somewhere to learn much about how the Germanish folks of elder times thought about the world. From what I've read, it seems that it is very much an outgrowth of its time, and holds a lot of the ways of thinking of the late 19th and early 20th century. All that being said, it seems to be a thoroughgoing take on getting at the thoughtworld of pre-Christian Germanish folks, rather than only understanding their tales or folkways.

English Heroic Legends

By: Kathleen Herbert
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Herbert has written tales about heroes from English lore, told again in a way that is meant to draw on what we know about Elder times and that makes sense and reads well to today's readers, rather than the often patchy bits of poetry or mentions in the Chronicle or the like.

Trees in Anglo-Saxon England

By: Della Hooke
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: This seems to be what it says on the tin: a thoroughgoing look at how trees were written about, crafted, and so forth amongst the Anglo-Saxons. I don't think it homes in on worship or belief most of all, but trees were very weighty in Anglo-Saxon belief, even in Christian times (see "The Dream of the Rood" for a showdeal, which is a poem about the crucifixion the way the tree that became the cross saw it).

Odin - The One-Eyed God and the (Indo) Germanic Mannerbunde

By: Kris Kershaw
Have I Read It?: Yes
Thoughts: This is maybe the most insightful book on Odin and where his historical worship came from and how it changed. I haven't read widely enough to know how much weight to give to those who set aside Kershaw's claims, but much was made clear to me through reading this, and I still need to meditate on it more.

Demons and Spirits of the Land

By: Claude Lecouteux
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Lecouteux is a French thinker who has pulled together much work on "folklore" rather than on the writings of elder times above, which makes him a helpful complement. Many of his books have only been translated into English in the last ten years or so, so I think he hasn't hit the English-speaking world as hard as the amount and worth of his work maybe should. I'm still only dipping my toes into his stuff, but it is wonderful to find someone talking about things I want to learn about, but coming at it from a wholly different grounding than what I already know.

Encyclopedia of Norse and Germanic Folklore, Mythology, and Magic

By: Claude Lecouteux
Have I Read It?: Much of it
Thoughts: This is Lecouteux's book I've read the most of so far. It helps shore up some of the gaps in Simek's Dictionary of Northern Mythology, again, with far more weight given to folklore.

The Hidden History of Elves and Dwarfs

By: Claude Lecouteux
Have I Read It?: Bits
Thoughts: Another book that I started in on and then had to set aside for my year of reading only books by dead writers. Even though the name of this book has the ugly "dwarfs" instead of the lovely and rightful "dwarves", I am very much looking forward to digging back into this book. It seems to shed a lot of light on why it is that elves and dwarves have something to do with barrows and forebearers, which is something I look forward to working out my own take on.

The Tradition of Household Spirits

By: Claude Lecouteux
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Another of Lecouteux's with bearing on my own belief and worship, as I would like to build strong bonds with the wights of my own house.

Norse Mythology

By: John Lindow
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Still another "overview" book, I don't know much about Lindow, but his research seems to include the beliefs and worship of the folks around the Germanish, such as the Sami and Inuit, which might give him some insight into things that shaped their belief outside of the Proto-Indo-European wellspring.

Heathen Gods in Old English Literature

By: Richard North
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: This work goes through where the pre-Christian Gods are named in the Old English writings, and seems to make the case that Ing (likely the same as Ingvi-Freyr) held a very high standing in the beliefs and worship of the Anglo-Saxons. Some folks seem to think he might read too much into what is there, but as I said above, I'd rather have folks speculating wildly than not sharing insights that might turn out worthwhile.

Rites and Religions of the Anglo-Saxons

By: Gale R. Owen
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: It sounds like this is a pretty thorough look at the wide field of evidence for what the Anglo-Saxons believed and how they worshipped. Unlike many books in this field, it also seems this one doesn't hold itself only to Germanish many-godded (polytheist) worship, but also looks at Roman and Celtic many-godded worship and Christianity.

The Elder Gods

By: Stephen Pollington
Have I Read It?: Much of it
Thoughts: Pollington has become one of my best-loved writers on all things Anglo-Saxon. His work is thorough, with wide-faring citations and careful bringing together of widespread threads. He also strikes a balance between sharing what implications a bit of evidence might have, while being careful to call out what is well-grounded and what is more out-there. This book does a wonderful job of finding the links between the Old English evidence (often scant) and the Nordic lore that is so much more thorough. If your interest in Germanish lore, belief, or worship is at all Anglo-Saxon flavored, this book is well worth your time.

Leechcraft

By: Stephen Pollington
Have I Read It?: Some
Thoughts: All of the good things I say about The Elder Gods hold true here as well, but this time in a book about the healing ways of the Anglo-Saxons, which were made up of wortlore (herbalism), eating right, charms, prayer, and other fields. Amongst other things, has a thorough run through of the "Nine Herbs Charm" and the whole body in Old English and New of three main works: the Lacnunga, Bald's Third Leechbook, and The Old English Herbarium.

Essays on Germanic Religion

By: Edgar C. Polome
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Written by the co-founder of the Journal of Indo-European Studies, Polome was a long-time professor at my own alma mater, The University of Texas at Austin. As his journal name might make you think, Polome's strength is bringing insights from comparative Indo-European work to bear on Germanish belief, to help fill in gaps and unriddle things that make us scratch our heads in the lore.

The Turnip Princess

By: Franz Xaver von Schonwerth (Translated by Erika Eichenseer)
Have I Read It?: Bits
Thoughts: Another gathering of fairy tales, this was put together by another German at the same time as the Grimms were doing their work - they all knew each other - but Schonwerth's work ended up in a small town's bookhoard until the early 21st century, when it was translated and published. I find it most helpful for giving me another way of looking at the better known fairy tales, as many of the tales in this work are almost familiar, but unalike enough to strike you as weird.

Dictionary of Northern Mythology

By: Rudolf Simek
Have I Read It?: Much of it

At times not quite as thorough as I might hope for, it still makes for a great springboard into other works on any given entry. Fair warning: so that entries might be brief, Simek sometimes gives his own thoughts as if they are settled even when learned folks might still be thrashing it out.

Works on the Proto-Indo-Europeans

While not straightforwardly about Germanish belief, these works look into what we might know or guess about the forebearers that Germanish folks shared with other folks of Eurasia. These can be a wellspring of surprising insights, but should also be taken with the right amount of salt, for almost the whole field comes from folks going "this thing calls this other thing to mind", whether that be words that sound alike, tales that go along the same lines, or Gods that took similar worship. Professional men and women of learning work very hard to put these likenesses on the strongest footing they might, but at the end of the day, they make a call on what they deem believable and what not, so be careful taking any of their conclusions without giving them your own thought. Mark that if you start going down this path, then works of lore for every other Indo-European folk might have a bearing on what you can work out, but I've deemed those out of scope for this list, lest it grow to even more laughable length.

The Horse, the Wheel, and Language

By: David W. Anthony
Have I Read It?: Yes
Thoughts: A wonderful pulling-together of everything we had at the time this was printed on "where did the speakers of proto-Indo-European come from?" A lot of the genetic evidence hadn't been published yet, but this pulls together linguistic, archaeological, and comparative means to argue that the speakers of Proto-Indo-European came from the steppe just north of the Black Sea (modern-day Ukraine and Western Russia). Also a great example of how to make a multi-disciplinary argument thoroughly and fairly straightforwardly.

In Search of the Indo-Europeans

By: J.P. Mallory
Have I Read It?: Yes
Thoughts: An earlier take on putting together the comparative evidence for the Proto-Indo-Europeans, this gives a bit more of a picture (based on speculation, of course) of what the Proto-Indo-Europeans looked like.

Mitra-Varuna

By: Georges Dumezil
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: Dumezil first put forward the now widely-held thought that the speakers of Proto-Indo-European had a three part society: Wizard-Priest-Kings, Warriors, and Makers, and that these three "functions" show up in the mythology of the folks that grew out of the Proto-Indo-Europeans. This take meets with some skepticism, but I haven't ever read it for myself to weigh it, which I ought to do.

How to Kill a Dragon

By: Calvert Watkins
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: A book of "comparative poetics", rather than focusing only on similar thematic elements in the myths of unalike Indo-European folks, Watkins looks at the likenesses in how these folks came up with poems (mostly oral, but later, written down). He argues that you can arrive at a Proto-Indo-European myth about killing a dragon, that had common poetic elements that left traces for hundreds of years after.

The Family of the Gods

By: William G. Davey
Have I Read It?: Yes
Thoughts: Unluckily, this seems hard to find these days - not all of the volumes were available on Amazon for Kindle last I looked. These are a fairly in-depth look at the kinship links between Gods/Goddesses/heroes and so forth across many Indo-European folks, looking to land on a myth we might understand through familial research. Helpful for fleshing out some corners of thought, but maybe not as carefully researched as some others.

The Indo-Europeans

By: Jean Haudry
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: A take on summarizing what we think we can know about the speakers of Proto-Indo-European culturally, linguistically, religiously, and so forth. Any such work will have a lot of speculation.

Other Works with Thinner Links

These works are not straightforwardly about Germanish belief, but have links or likenesses that might be worth digging into anyhow.

The History of Religious Ideas

By: Mircea Eliade
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: A beast of a four-part series, Eliade draws upon a great wealth of stories, belief, and so forth from very unalike folks, lands, and ways of worshiping to try to give an overall look at what belief/worship look like around the world and through time. His biggest problem seems to be wanting to squeeze everything into a sequence of "primitive –> better –> best".

The Sacred and the Profane

By: Mircea Eliade
Have I Read It?: No
Thoughts: One of Eliade's foundational works, but much shorter than his mammoth four part series, I've been meaning to read this for years. Lays out what seems to be common amongst folks of very different lands and ways of worship for what counts as "hallowed" and what as "to do with normal life".

Shamanism

By: Mircea Eliade
Have I Read It?: About halfway.
Thoughts: Eliade uses "shamanism" fairly broadly and looks at what he thinks might have been the common way of working with Gods and spirits before "religions" arose. World Full of Gods

By: John Michael Greer

The Shaman: Patterns of Siberian and Ojibway Healing

Thoughts on Spending a Year with the Dead

Date: 2023-January-15

The Rules

For all of 2022, I set myself the goal of reading only books by writers who had died before I began reading it, unless the book was 1) needed for work, 2) about a hands-on skill that I put to work in 2022, or 3) written by a friend or kin.

I'm glad I did it, I think it was good for me, and now I'd like to share a bit about how it went and what I learned.

What Went Well

First off, I met my goal, so huzzah! Since I made this boast publicly and told my friends and family about it, it helped very much whenever I found myself wanting to bend or break my rules. One of my goals for the year was to read more old books, and this helped me do so. I also wanted to use it as a spur to read more "great" or otherwise intimidating books, and it helped me there as well - among others, I read Spengler's The Decline of the West (both volumes) and Goethe's Faust.

This also drove me to think more about my reading and where I might look to answer a question - if I wanted to read about a something, I had to try to find a book about it by someone who was dead. Brand new field like the blockchain? Too bad, maybe this isn't the year for cutting edge stuff.

When I found myself wanting to read books by living authors, I had to stew on it for a while. Sometimes I found that my yearning for a book cooled after a few weeks, but with a handful, I was literally counting the days to the New Year. I've already read two short books I'd been wanting to get to for months now, and it felt like a real treat to get to them at last.

Another of my main goals was to open myself up to ways of thinking not tangled up in the trends and prejudices of today. Sure enough, these books were at times refreshing, shocking, or both.

Altogether, I think this was great New Year's goal. It could bend to fit my changing tastes, needs at work, and even having a baby. On the other hand, it was spelled out well enough to make it easy to know if I was following it or not and binding enough to get me to act differently than I would have without it. If any of that grabs you, consider giving a shot sometime.

What Might Have Gone Better

While I think the goal was a good one and the year went well, there were a few less-thrilling things. First off, I read a lot less: 17 books against 46 in 2021 (not including comics). To be fair to my goal, I don't think it's the only, or even the main, driver of why I read less - I would put that more on a larger teaching load, long books to read, a new note-taking system, and most of all, having a new baby for the last few months of the year. I also got deeper into my spiritual practice and took up a bit of leatherworking over the summer, so there were those as well.

Also, and this one might be thanks to my goal, it was much harder for me to get into most of the fiction I chose. When I really love a novel, I'll pick it up whenever I get a spare moment, whereas if I merely like it, it'll only get 10-15 minutes before bed, if I don't just fall face first into bed. I also missed comics and graphic novels. It seems my tastes run much more with the times with fiction than non-fiction. Of course, there are lots of comics whose authors have died and every kind of fiction imaginable, but for whatever reason, this bit was less successful for me.

I also could have set my rules more clearly for edge cases: does looking up an entry in a reference book count? How about finding a saying from something I've already read? Or checking a book's index for a key word? How about the book I'm getting meditation themes from? Or an art book where I'm looking at the pictures?

I ended up settling on "referencing" not counting as "reading", and a small amount of skimming to get the gist of a book was okay, as long as I didn't linger too long on any bits. I counted meditation themes as "learning a hands-on skill" and looking at art books didn't count as "reading", though it was a good reminder to go look for older stuff instead of only looking at the latest art.

I felt like all of this was in keeping with my why I set the goal, which was mostly for books I would read cover-to-cover but I would have felt better about it if I wasn't asking myself if I was "cheating."

Likely the closest I came to fudging the goal was spending a lot of time reading blogs. Maybe too much time. Maybe time that would have been given to books if not for the goal.

For example, I read the whole archive of Unqualified Reservations and Gray Mirror by Curtis Yarvin, the former of which includes many series that have been sold as e-books. I ended up counting it as okay so long as I read it "as a blog" - in my browser, post-by-post, and in the order they were posted, not how they were put together for publication.

This was more a case of sticking to the letter of my goal while bending the spirit a fair bit - I spent hours and hours reading long-form prose by living folks, about the same things I would have read books about if not for my goal. So, as much as the goal was meant to take away distractions so I could read stuff I might otherwise put off, reading blogs so much went against that.

The Open-Minded Materialist's Gentle Introduction to Spirituality

Date: 2022-January-08

I've always liked learning about mythology and religion, but most of my tries at prayer or worship left me cold. I gave it a shot for a bit in college, but by a few years ago, I had pretty well settled into a scientific materialistic atheism, or at least a pretty hard-edged agnosticism - I thought there was nothing reasonable you could say about things that weren't available to the five senses, so why speculate. Then, much to my surprise, about a year and a half ago (mid 2021), I had what seems to have been a pretty classic religious experience. Since then, I've been taking a new look at much of how I think about the world, and it's been a wild ride. At this point, I can still remember how the world looked as a materialist, but now I see things differently. Standing astride these two worldviews, I think I might be able to help out anyone looking across that chasm and wondering what's on the other side, and if it's worth finding a way over.

This Post is for Those Who Feel Something is Lacking

The reader I have in mind for this post is someone with a pretty firm grounding in how the world works - likely you know a basic outline of history, some understanding of physics and biology, and enough psychology that folks' day-to-day foibles don't surprise you too much. Maybe one of these fields grabs you more, and you've gone deeper there, but basically, you don't look at history, or technology, or politics and feel totally perplexed. Things may not be where you'd like, but they at least make sense. Amongst all of that, though, something's under-developed: a sense of meaning. Very likely you've read some philosophy, some self-help, maybe you've tried out meditation or gratitude journaling or the like. If your mostly-good-enough worldview falls short precisely at the most important questions ("what's it all about?", "what should I do with my life?", and so forth), then I hope this post might help you out.

By "Spiritual", I Mean Whatever Makes Life Worth Living

Before we go further, I wanted to pin down what I mean by "spiritual," since I'll be talking about it a lot below. Trying to define "spiritual" or "spirituality" is notoriously tough, so I'm going to go with an open-ended definition for this post. When I say "spiritual" (in this post at least), what I mean is whatever beliefs, experiences, and practices that strengthen the feeling that life is fundamentally good and worth living over the long-term. That last bit is important, because otherwise we might rope in a lot of short-term enjoyable experiences, like drugs or casual sex, that few folks would give much weight to as "spiritually significant". Also, I use the word "strengthen", because I don't think many things are well-served by a binary of "spiritual/not-spiritual." Maybe having an enchanting encounter with an attractive member of your preferred sex is "spiritual" if you've been feeling cold and lonely, but it is likely to be less spiritually weighty than building a life-long partnership with someone and raising a family. So, for my definition, "spiritual" is continuous, not categorical.

One Way to Go About Things

Start From Where You Are

The most important thing on the spiritual path is to take your own experience seriously. Questions of meaning and spiritual significance are deeply personal, and your answers are likely to be even more so. First, take stock of why you're even asking these questions - are you feeling bitter, depressed, or angry? Is life going "fine", but it feels like something is missing? Are there niggling little edges of your model of the world that don't quite work? Have you had an experience you can't wholly explain? Wherever you're starting, be honest with yourself. Folks often use "I'm just being honest" as an excuse to be overly harsh, even towards themselves. So don't do that, but also don't try to pretend everything is fine if it isn't.

Wherever you and your beliefs are, though, there are very likely things you can do to move your life in a toward something you like better. If you just can't take religious claims as anything other than wishful thinking for stupid people, if you try to pray or do rituals, it's going to feel hollow and dumb. So, if that's you, don't start there. Maybe something like reading philosophy and meditating on it could work for you. Maybe even that feels too "woo woo" for you: if so, try looking for some scientifically validated practices like gratitude journaling or getting regular exercise. If you feel like your mental/emotional state could be better, you're almost certainly right, but your path to get there is unlikely to be the same as anyone else's. Down the way, it might take you to some funky places, but for now, you have to start from where you are.

Find Credible Folks Rather than Believable Claims

Okay, let's imagine that you've taken a good, hard look at your life and what you believe about it, and you're thinking something like "this isn't quite working for me."

So, you settle on widening your outlook, and you start reading some folks outside of your comfort zone - self-help gurus, philosophers, religious folks, maybe even occultists. As you do, you run into things that leave you scratching your head at best or scoffing in disbelief at worst. One of the pitfalls of spiritual writers, especially alternative spiritual writers, is a whole lot of "woo". If you're coming from any kind of rationalist background, it's easy to run into stuff that you just can't take seriously at all. They are operating from an utterly different epistemological/metaphysical basis, and they take for granted things about the world that seem obviously wrong to you. You end up finding yourself in a wilderness of wild claims about how the world works and what might make your life better. Let's take a common example: most religious folks believe in the actual, external reality of one or more sentient beings that happen not to have bodies (Gods, angels, spirits, and so forth). If you are coming from a materialist viewpoint, this claim is patently nonsense. Those religious people then claim that talking to some of these non-physical beings can make your life much better. So, how do you handle this recommendation to start praying?

Here's my take: look for writers that you find credible about something, and work on holding off judgment on the weirder, woo-woo-ier stuff they talk about. Look for fields where their judgment and reasoning make sense to you. Over time, you might find yourself going "this person is smart, grounded, and reasonable about just about everything, why does he believe this crazy stuff?" With a truly open mind, that's a helpful stead to find yourself in. If you think about it, you pretty much do this all the time - everyone you know has a quirky set of beliefs about the world, some of which you'll hold with and some you won't. For one easy example, I'm willing to bet you have someone among your friends or kinsfolk that you like and think well of, but with whom you will not talk politics.

So, when weighing the worth of spiritual writers, look at those of their beliefs about the world which you can more readily evaluate. Do they talk about history, or politics, or psychology, or literature, or other non-spiritual matters? Do they make sense when they do? The goal here is to ask yourself "does this person seem trustworthy overall?" If they seem nuts about everything, chances are good they're nuts about spiritual matters too. On the other hand, if they seem like calm, thoughtful folks with a clear outlook on the world, and yet they happen to believe something that seems obviously impossible to you, well, now that's interesting, isn't it? These are the folks you'll want to pay attention to.

"By Their Fruits Ye Shall Know Them"

Since the definition we're using for spirituality here grounds out in how you live your life, there's a helpful rule of thumb you can apply to would-be spiritual teachers that comes from one of the best-known spiritual teachers of all time, Jesus: "by their fruits ye shall know them." In other words, look at what kind of outcomes this guy has in his own life. How does he treat others? Is he surrounded by scandal? Is his life a raging dumpster fire? It's always helpful to think through these questions when taking someone's advice, but it's even more weighty for philosophical or spiritual things, since their whole point is making your life better. Look out for folks who seem to make sense, but whose lives are a mess.

Consciousness is Not Available to the Five Senses

One rationalist argument that I always found convincing was that you can't meaningfully reason about things that are not available to the five senses. In other words, if there are things about the world that we can't experience with our senses, we have know way of knowing about them for sure, so we shouldn't bother speculating. Further, since the world can seemingly be explained pretty darn well by those things that are available to the senses, it's very likely extraneous to assume there's anything else. This post by Eric S. Raymond is a good example of that kind of reasoning.

I was pretty comfortable with such reasoning until I read A World Full of Gods by John Michael Greer. He made a point that blew a big hole in that comfortable line of reasoning. We do in fact have an important element of experience that is not available to the five senses: the contents of our consciousness. Thoughts, imaginations, emotions. These are obviously important parts of our experience, but they are not accessible to anyone's senses. The typical materialist explanation is that these experiences are just epiphenomena of the matter in our skulls, but that's just an assumption. If there's one hugely important area of our experience that is not amenable to the five senses, then there might be others. One areas worth giving some consideration is one that every single human culture we have a record of (except ours recently) has found self-evident: the reality of spiritual beings.

Reason is a Helpful Tool, but Some Things are Subjective

What all of the above led me to is that reason is an incredibly helpful tool, but it's not the end-all, be-all for understanding the world and our experience of it. If you begin any kind of spiritual practice, even just journaling or meditating, you will run into a lot of experiences that you can't verify or validate with other folks - they will be wholly subjective to you. Some of those wholly subjective experiences might feel tremendously important, but you won't be able to confirm them empirically or through formal rationality. Does that mean you should ignore them or discount them?

Practice Beats Belief

Okay, the above points were about the sources you check out and how you think about them, but this one is for you. From my own experience, what you think is true about the world is much less important than what you do. You might believe that it's great to treat everybody with loving-kindness, but if you keep acting like a dick, that's not worth much. I have some thoughts on what practices might be most helpful, but the core idea is regular practice - pick something you think will be good for you to do, and then do it every day. Meditation is a good choice, but there are others - like a gratitude journal, volunteering, yoga, prayer, and more. The okay thing you will do regularly is way better than the great thing you give up on.

You May Not Be Able to Make Pascal's Wager

Some folks say that you should give spiritual practices a shot even if you don't believe their "really real." Something like "pray even if you don't think anyone is listening." In my case, a few years back, I was convinced that rituals and spiritual practices were a useful way to get in touch with the archetypes of the unconscious, but I thought it was "all in my head." It seems that this works for some folks (see this post from Eric S. Raymond for one example). So I tried it out - I called on Gods, did rituals, and so forth, but all while believing their "reality" was something wholly metaphorical. I felt silly. I couldn't get past feeling like I was talking to myself, so even though I kept at it for a while, it never stopped feeling like an empty waste of time. All of this is to say, if you can't get yourself to at least thinking "maybe there's somebody/something else out there," you might not have much luck with more explicitly religious practices like prayer, and maybe you should start with something else, like meditation.

Where You Might Start

As I've stressed above, any grappling with spiritual matters you do will be deeply your own, and thus what works for you won't be what works for others. That being said, it can be hard to know where to start, so I'd like to share some ways of thinking about meaning that I have found helpful along the way.

Existentialism Says to Make Your Own Meaning

I've listed existentialism first, as it begins with a presupposition wholly in keeping with modern materialism: nothing is meaningful in and of itself. Instead, it is up to you to work out what is meaningful to you and why that matters. It can be a harsh pill to swallow, and it's one that I have since found doesn't meet my own spiritual needs, but existential thinkers tend to approach the problems with meaning with brutal clarity and how to tackle them with admirable courage. The single best work here has got to be Man's Search for Meaning by Viktor Frankl. Nothing quite like a holocaust survivor's tale to put life's problems in perspective.

Self-Help Shows You How to Get Things Done

I spent years very into self-help stuff, mostly stuff by Tim Ferriss, Ryan Holiday, Mark Manson, and so forth. I learned a lot about myself and a lot of helpful ways of dealing with stress, staying healthy, and being productive. Even so, you might say that I wouldn't even be writing this post if I had found the meaning I was looking for in this field. Most self-help seeks to appeal to a wide audience, and so takes few stands on deeper matters of meaning, instead either holding itself to smaller, more pragmatic things ("get in shape!" or "manage your finances!") or else grounds out in vague, woo-woo stuff of the "do what feels right to you!" kind.

The other big flaw in a lot of self-help is that it's mostly about solving problems. Don't get me wrong, solving problems is great, but it's not always the right way to come at things. Is grief that your mother died a "problem" to be solved? If every Sunday night you have a gnawing sense of dread, does that mean you should learn the best strategy for getting a promotion, or might it be a token of something deeper? All that being said, I think The War of Art by Steven Pressfield is very worthwhile, and Tim Ferriss's work is still top notch for learning how to get real world stuff done.

Stoic Philosophy Tells You to Take the Reins of Your Feelings

Unlike Existentialism, Stoic philosophy still pulls a lot of weight in my outlook. It is often pitched as fully in keeping with materialist belief, which it largely is, due to one of its main teachings: you can't control what happens in the world around you, but you can control how you think about it. Put enough work into steering how you think about what happens to you, and you can begin steering how you feel about things. Already, that's pretty worthwhile, and nothing about it goes against a materialist outlook.

The other main teaching of the stoics follows from this and is a bit harder to square with materialism, but you can if you wrangle it hard enough: since you can not control the world around you, no outside measures of "a good life" can withstand everything that might happen. Instead, you should steer your thoughts (and feelings) by "virtue". The reason this is harder for a materialist is that it turns out it's really hard, maybe even impossible, to come up with a truly bullet proof definition of "virtue" on purely empirical grounds. You can certainly get to good enough for living your life, but if you're looking for epistemic certainty, it can leave some gnawing doubts. The classical stoics didn't have this problem, because they believed virtue came from a universal divine source.

If you want to get started with Stoic philosophy, there are two good options: 1) Meditations by Marcus Aurelius and 2) Letters from a Stoic by Seneca. Meditations was the personal diary of a thoughtful and disciplined man, whereas Letters from a Stoic was the avuncular advice of a successful mentor helping a younger friend work out his life. You can't really go wrong with either, but Meditations comes off more like maxims and helpful self-talk, whereas Letters from a Stoic is more conversational and more understanding of the occasional misstep. Pick whichever of those grabs you more.

Meaningness Explores the Ambiguity of "Meaning"

This is an online "book" that tries to tackle the problem of meaning, meaninglessness, and everything in between from a materialist-friendly Buddhist stand point. I think he does about as good a job as you possibly can if you take materialism as a key assumption. The heart of his argument is that most religious approaches put too much meaning on every damn thing, whereas nihilism puts too little meaning on stuff that everyone sees as meaningful unless you stop and think too hard about it. Instead, his take is that "meaning" is something rather ambiguous that all humans experience, partly by creating it and partly by responding to things in the outside world. Thorough, well-argued, and helpfully nuanced, maybe the best thing this book does is to make a telling, materialist-compatible case that nihilism is dumb and rationalism isn't an unassailable metaphysical/epistemological truth.

An adjacent set of writing I have found helpful is sam[]zdat by Lou Keep. This blog begins with the same intellectual and cultural starting places as the wider "Rationalist" community, but wrestles much more with how far scientific evidence and logical reasoning can go when it comes to values.

Religion Might Take You Some Places You Didn't Know About

Or, maybe you've come a bit farther than I've been thinking, and you're at least open to the thought that all of this "spiritual" stuff is not only metaphorical, not just a pithy way to sum up thorny psychological, sociological, and cultural factors, but instead there might be something outside of yourself that you'd be better off coming to know. Given who I reckon is reading this, it's fairly likely that when I say "religion," what comes to mind is something that looks a lot like Protestant Christianity - either in its Fundamentalist Evangelical flavor or its Progressive Social Justice flavor. Either that, or some kind of westernized Buddhism, as I have heard humorously, but not wrongly, called "Richard Gere Buddhism". Notwithstanding my less-than-kind likenesses, if any of that grabs you, by all means, go forth and delve into that thing!

For this post, though, I'm not going to say much on these, since I think you likely wouldn't be here if the ready-to-hand religious options did much for you. Instead, I'd like to draw your awareness to some very unalike choices. You see, religion of some kind is found amongst the folks of all lands, but between those lands, there are wildly unalike ways to deal with the spiritual world, many of which you might not have given much thought to. Here's a handful:

  • Does Christianity hold some weight with you, but the kinds you know don't work for you? Consider poking around "Tradcath" (Traditional Catholic) or Orthodox Christianity. C.S. Lewis and G.K. Chesterton are some worthwhile writers here, and there are loads of writings, podcasts, and so forth to check out online, but it's not a field I've looked deeply into, so I don't know which to steer you toward.
  • What if you can't get the claims of monotheism to make sense? The afore-said World Full of Gods does a wonderful job of spelling out why polytheism is worth taking earnestly, but if you'd like to give it a try yourself, check out The Soul's Inner Statues and pair with whatever mythology most speaks to you.
  • India and Japan have thriving home-grown religions in the shape of Hinduism and Shintoism/Buddhism (they often run together in Japan). Both have a very wide set of practices, prayers, rituals, and so forth, most of which are deeply linked with the neighborhoods from which they spring - so much so that you likely need to go and see them in the flesh to fully get them, but if something grabs you from afar, why not at least read about it?
  • Or how about the religious thinking and folkways of the sundry indigenous folks around the world? God is Red by Vine Deloria, Jr. is a great starter for Native American Indian spirituality, and Sand Talk by Tyson Yunkaporta is one for Australian aboriginal people.
  • If you're really quirky, you might think about looking into some of the West's alternative spiritual paths of the last few hundred years - Theosophy, Occultism, the Druidry Revival, and so forth.

I've shared all of the above since I think a lot of folks feel like their lives are lacking spiritually, but the [options] they know about leave them cold. If neither megachurch Christianity nor Yoga-studio Buddhism do it for you, it turns out there's a lot more out there, some of which just might grab you.

Heed Your Own Experience

Whatever you find on your spiritual journey, remember that it is your journey. If religious or spiritual practices aren't working for you, then hey, they're not what you need right here and right now. On the other hand, if you find them worthwhile, don't let the fact that they seem weird or don't make sense to other people stop you from doing what works for you. It's hard enough to deal with your own doubts and those of your friends and kin if you find yourself drawn to Christianity or Buddhism or other widely-practiced religions, but it's even tougher if you find yourself drawn to something more out there. Praying to multiple Gods? Lighting incense? Putting on funny robes and walking around in circles? You might find yourself feeling a bit self-conscious, or worse. Whatever you do, the whole point of spiritual practice is to make better sense of yourself and your experience, and to enrich your life, so pay attention to how your practice makes you feel and what impact it has on your life, and don't let any hangups get in the way of that.

It might turn out to be a long, weird ride, but it's worth it.

Rules of Germanish Meter

Date: 2022-November-20

I'm writing this post as much as a reference for me and as a place to point to whenever I make use of traditional Germanish metrical forms as anything else. For all of this, I am greatly indebted to Eirik Westcoat who has written a great deal of poetry in modern English using these forms for Heathen religious purposes, and lays out the rules very clearly in his book Viking Poetry for Heathen Rites. Note that this post has nothing to say about the other great thread in Germanish poetic tradition, which is the use of formal, stylized metaphors known as kennings, like the "whale road" for the sea or "flame of battle" for a sword.

Rules Common to All Germanish Meters

Once you start learning the meters given below, it becomes clear that they are all variations on a theme. That "theme" was likely an ancestral form of poetry practiced by the speakers of Proto-Germanic that speakers of all of the Germanish languages took with them and changed over time. The most complex forms are all from Old Norse, which retained an unbroken native language poetic tradition until the latest time. I find it easier to remember a few key "lego pieces" that all of the meters are made of:

  • Alliteration and syllable counts are the main focus of Germanish meters - other poetic considerations like rhyming, placement of stress, and so forth are sometimes found, but distinctly secondary in importance
  • Only stressed syllables are counted for alliteration. In today's English, this is usually the first syllable, but not always, and it can be helpful to consult a dictionary, where stress is usually indicated with a "`" or similar symbol before the stressed syllable. Prefixes, like on-, be-, up-, un-, and so forth are often not stressed.
  • Note that the above allows a lot of flexibility in stress placement, compared to Latin and Greek poetic forms, but you do still get some interesting rhythm from following the rules given below
  • Consonant clusters that are pronounced as a single sound (th, ch, sk, sp, and so forth) alliterate with each other, rather than with the initial letter used in spelling. For example, "spelling" does not alliterate with "see", but does alliterate with "speak".
  • All vowels alliterate with each other (or "assonate" with each other if you want to be picky)

Rules for Fornyrdhislag - "Old Song Meter"

This was one of the most common verse forms in Old Norse, and given its similarity to attested Anglo-Saxon and continental Germanish verse forms, it's likely pretty close to the Proto-Germanic kind of poetry from which all of these kinds sprang. Most of the other verse forms can be told of as changes to this one.

  • Usually written in eight-line stanzas
  • The main unit of composition is the short line/half line, which must have 2 stressed syllables and usually has 4+ total syllables
  • The alliteration is between pairs of these short lines (A and B), where the first stressed syllable of the second short line (B1) alliterates with either or both of the first or second stressed syllable of the first short line (A1 or A2)
  • The second stressed syllable of the second short line (B2) must not alliterate with the first stressed syllable in that short line (B1), but it may optionally alliterate with either of the stressed syllables in the first short line (A1 OR A2). Due to the above rule, this means that if B2 alliterates, it may only alliterate with whichever A syllable B1 does not alliterate with

Fornyrdhislag Example

O wise Woden,
Well-helped you are
by ravens quick-flying,
Flying around,
and steed eight-legged,
Stepping lively.

Rules for Anglo-Saxon Long Lines

So far, this is the meter I have the most experience with, as it is fairly straightforward and bendsome, as well as coming from the same background as the Gods and Lore that I draw on the most. Almost the same as Fornyrdhislag, but with these changes:

  • Usually, two "half lines" are written/printed together to form the single "long line" of the verse name. These follow the same alliteration rules as the pairs of short lines in fornyrdhislag
  • Half lines must have 4+ syllables each
  • Write as many lines as needed to tell what you're trying to say - they are not broken into stanzas

Anglo-Saxon Long Lines Example

Standing at shoulder, | with shield steadfast,
Battle we welcome | with warriors bold.

Rules for Galdralag - "Magic Spell Meter"

This ones a bit trickier than either of the above, but relies on the same fundamentals as the above.

  • This comes in stanzas of four lines
  • The first two lines follow the same rules as the short lines from fornyrdhislag, though the first line can be as short as two or three syllables
  • The last two lines are longer, with three stressed syllables, any two of which alliterate
  • The last line often, but not always, is a slight variation on line 3

Galdralag Example

O spellweavers,
Wounded by spears,
Lost lives for wisdom
Wakened well for lore.

Other Meters

There are other meters, some of which get pretty complicated, and I may add them to this post as I get experience with them, but I find the above give a pretty good base for coming up with many kinds of poems for prayers and other ends.

Why a Heathen Rosary?

Date: 2022-September-25

As I mentioned last time, I thought I had put forth what I was up to coming up with a "Heathen Rosary", but it turns out I hadn't, so I thought "why not now?"

First off, it might be helpful to say what I mean by a "Heathen Rosary", since that could mean rather a few unalike things. In Pagan Prayer Beads by John Michael Greer and 'Clare Vaughn' (his wife's pen name for this work), they use "rosary" for any set of prayers tied to a set of prayer beads. Galina Krasskova "retooled" the rosary with a set of prayers meant to be said on a set of beads made for the Christian Rosary and with the intent of keeping much of the rhythm of the Christian prayer, because she grew up around it and it was a foundational part of her early exposure to religious practice. My own take is somewhere in between these. I have never been Catholic, and the Rosary has never been a part of my spiritual practice. That being said, through prayer, meditation, and divination, I got the impression that I ought to come up with a prayer that takes advantage of much of what works well for the Christian Rosary, while still being customized for my own beliefs.

Basically, I got the idea I ought to work out a prayer that is "like the rosary", and to make that happen, I decided I ought to learn more about the Christian Rosary. As such, I've been reading through the prayers of Rosary and some works about it. From this reading, the thing that has struck me most strongly is the Rosary's alternation between familiarity/repetition and novelty. Also, the Christian Rosary has a strong alternation of praying to the masculine and feminine aspects of the Catholic religion. So, I've been trying to work out how to get the best of these traits in a regular prayer without calling upon Christian religious figures.

So, what I've come to is the Framework for a Heathen Rosary. I'm still working out what the saying of intent and closing of each "group" of "Hail Iduns" should be, along with what the equivalent of the mysteries might be. Framework for a Heathen Rosary

Date: 2022-September-18

I started writing this post thinking that I had already introduced my goal of coming up with a "Heathen Rosary," but looking back, it seems I only briefly mentioned it in my "Hail Idun" post. So here's the short version: the Rosary is a longstanding contemplative prayer that has been spiritually valuable to a lot of folks all around the world and for a very long time. As such, it seems like a good model for coming up with a contemplative prayer in keeping with my own beliefs. Through prayer, meditation, and just plain thinking about it, I've come to a rough idea of what that prayer might look like, but I've still got a lot of work to do.

I had hoped to be further along in working this set of prayers out by this point, but since I'm not, I thought I'd share what I do have so far. I haven't written out any of the prayers other than the "Hail Idun" Prayer, but I've gotten some idea of the shape those prayers will take.

  1. A Saying of Intent
  2. Open each group of prayers with a prayer to Woden
  3. Say Nine "Hail Iduns"
  4. Close each group with a prayer that again affirms relatedness with the beings of the world
  5. Repeat for four groups
  6. Close with another saying of striving to live well

If you know the Christian Rosary, this pretty closely follows the framework there. That collection of prayers opens with the Apostle's Creed, has 5 groups of prayers (Decades), each of which begins with an "Our Father" and is made up of 10 repetitions of the "Hail Mary", and closes with a "Glory Be." At the end of the whole prayer is a closing prayer. The big difference in my group of prayers, other than praying to different spiritual beings, is that the opening and closing are less statements of belief, and more a statement of intent. For Christianity, stating what you believe to be true about the world is an important foundation of religious practice. Germanish religion, on the other hand, like most many-Godded beliefs, puts far more weight on what you do and how you relate to the Gods. As such, rather than a "creed", I plan to open with a statement of intention to live well and do right by the Gods, and for the closing prayer to reiterate this idea.

Having nine "Hail Iduns" was a pretty obvious choice, given the prominence of 9 as a holy number in Germanish belief, but it was harder to figure out how many groups of prayers to say. I've come down on four groups for now, mostly because it is pretty close to the 5 groups of the Christian Rosary, but there are more instances of "fours" in Germanish myth than "fives". I haven't yet worked out how many groups of themes for contemplation (the "Mysteries" in the Christian tradition) to use, but I'm leaning toward using myths as the themes, and possibly grouping into three groups, such as the "Wyrd Mysteries, Werthende Mysteries, and the Schuld Mysteries."

Another element that I think is quietly implicit in the Christian Rosary but that I intend to emphasize pretty heavily is the alternation between prayers to a masculine power and to a feminine power. In Christianity, that's Jesus and Mary, and for me, that will mainly be Woden and Idun. I think that this interplay of masculine and feminine is essential to how this particular set of prayers works, but it might also be influenced by Christianity being otherwise so heavily oriented toward the masculine. The other complicating factor is that the Christian theological world is comparatively simple compared to most polytheistic understandings of the world - you cover pretty much all of your theological bases by affirming the Trinity and then praying to God the Father in the way Jesus taught, and by affirming Jesus's role even in the prayers to Mary.

For a polytheist, getting the same sense of praying to "everything overall" is much harder (and perhaps not needed?). In my case, I am seeing the unifying factor as the Tree of Life, Yggdrassil, and the relationships between the Gods, spirits, and other beings that it represents as what to call upon for "the whole holy world". I might also bring the Wyrds (Norns) into either the replacement for the "Glory Be" and/or the closing prayer. While specific divine beings, they are also embodiments of the workings of the universe and are connected with the Tree.

Right now, I'm planning on writing prayers for each of these pieces following a traditional Germanish meter (likely "Anglo-Saxon Long Lines"), and I'll share them here as I get them written. As always, any thoughts or feedback are welcome.

Looking Into the "Our Father"

Date: 2022-August-21

After my earlier post on the Catechism of the Council of Trent about the section "On Prayer", I went ahead and read through the rest of the section, which is a line-by-line treatment of the "Our Father". The catechism uses this prayer as an example of a single, sufficient prayer for Christian practice. While I am not a Christian, I find the thought of having one prayer that is a wholly sufficient baseline an especially interesting one, and I thought that it would be helpful to look in detail at what the Council of Trent had to say about the "Our Father".

Since I'm not a Christian, my main interest is in thinking through what crafting such a prayer for another faith might mean. Of course, I'm especially interested in coming up with such a prayer for my own faith, but to try to make this helpful for others, I'd like to generalize as much as possible. Lastly, though much of the prayer is particularly Christian, there is much within the prayer that is inspiring and beautiful for any spiritual path, and might be worth thinking through how to emulate in other faiths, and I'd like to highlight that as well.

The Progression of the Prayer

The Council of Trent broke down the prayer line-by-line and talked about what each line meant on its own, but also as a logical progression. The heart of it seems to be that you start universal, end universal, and have more particular asks sandwiched in-between. Each thing asked for also works both as a "plain" request for something obvious, and as a more sophisticated spiritual statement.

Our Father, Who Art in Heaven

The prayer begins by calling upon a loving father, not a harsh or distant lord. By specifying "our" father, the prayer also acknowledges that all men are brothers, and God is the father of all. Since Christians believe God to be everywhere, "Who art in Heaven" at first doesn't make much sense, but it serves to draw attention to the noblest part of visible nature, the sky, and asks those praying to compare that with God. More personally, by placing the highly personal figure of a "father" in the highly spiritual locale of "heaven", the prayer immediately establishes a link between personal practice and the transcendental goods sought through spiritual practice.

Hallowed Be Thy Name

The Council of Trent recognizes that God's name can be nothing but hallowed in an abstract sense, but instead frames this bit of the prayer as a recognition, and a plea by the one praying to treat the name of God as holy. More subtly, the Council argues that all things that are good and hallowed are encompassed by God's name, and that this part of the prayer recognizes that. It also serves as a reminder to those praying to try to bring holiness to God's name through their actions outside of prayer, which will serve as an encouragement to any folks whose belief might be wavering.

Thy Kingdom Come

The Council of Trent puts this forward as the highest possible good and says that it holds all other goods that might possibly be asked for - anything that might be truly good is actually a part of the Kingdom of God, and anything not a part of the Kingdom of God only seems good, but isn't truly so. This part of the prayer is meant to remind believers to notice how much better the goods of the Kingdom of Heaven are than the goods of this world, but also as an encouragement to bring as much of the Kingdom of God into the world as possible.

Thy Will Be Done

This is one that is seemingly at first the most strongly associated with purely Christian theology, yet that maybe has useful lessons for other faiths as well. The core idea is that the one praying ought to acknowledge that what he thinks he wants might actually not be such a great idea, and instead that he should gladly accept what the God prayed to believes to be best. Christians justify this position with original sin - because you are fundamentally flawed and cut off from the Divine, whatever you will to happen is tainted, and so you should instead seek the will of the higher power, who is untainted. Even without original sin, though, you can pretty readily get to the idea that what we think is best might not have all the needed information. The world is a big, complicated place, and we are just some moderately clever social apes - why on earth would we think that we know what's best for us? If you believe in the idea of a God or Gods at all, you should trust that they know a bit better than we do, from our highly limited material points of view.

On Earth as It Is in Heaven

This bit was especially interesting to me, as I learned something I hadn't ever considered before: the Council of Trent says that "On Earth as It Is in Heaven" applies equally to "Hallowed be Thy name," "Thy kingdom come," and "Thy will be done." I had always only read it as an extension of "Thy will be done" and nothing else. On the one hand, this clause acknowledges the contrast between that which is spiritual and that which is worldly, but on the other, it also works as a thanksgiving, to the degree that God's name is hallowed, His kingdom has come, and His will is done in this world. More broadly, it acknowledges the perfection of Heaven and the lack of perfection in the material world and expresses longing for the material world becoming more like the perfect spiritual world.

Give Us This Day Our Daily Bread

Here we at last come to the asking for day-to-day, bodily things. One of the points the Council of Trent makes is exactly that the asking for bodily things comes only after asking for the highest and most spiritual things. I also appreciate the amount of meaning they find in the few words in this line. Asking for "our" daily bread acknowledges that you have to work, but you also rely on God's help for anything good that comes into your life. Asking for "daily" bread acknowledges that you're asking for what you need everyday - necessities, not luxuries. "This day" acknowledges that what you had yesterday might not come automatically today - you can't take these things for granted and should keep on being thankful so long as you have what you need. Lastly, "Give us" rather than "give me" encourages the one praying to think not only of himself, but of the needs of all of his fellow men.

Even if you are not Christian, this is an impressive amount of religious meaning to pack into one line! I think it's maybe significant that we're really getting to the richest lines of the prayer when we turn from asking for high-level spiritual goods to normal, earthly needs.

Forgive Us Our Trespasses as We Forgive Those Who Trespass Against Us

This might be considered the heart of the Christian belief system, and might be the part of the prayer least easily adapted to other beliefs. Don't get me wrong, I think that forgiveness is an important part of many religious beliefs, but in Christianity, it is the belief, and so the line in the most common Christian prayer that most directly deals with forgiveness deserves a bit of attention. Once again, the plural subject ("forgive us our trespasses as we forgive those who trespass against us") does a lot of heavy lifting - this is not only asking for help with one's own forgiveness, but for all of mankind to get better at this very hard skill. Like other parts of the prayer, this one acknowledges the work done by both the one praying and God, in partnership. Lastly, one thought I found especially helpful from the Catechism was that folks might avoid saying the whole prayer, or at least this bit, if they had folks they couldn't yet forgive. In other words, if someone had someone against whom they were still nursing a grudge, they might hold off saying the "Our Father", or at least the line about forgiving trespasses. The Council of Trent very strongly came down on the belief that this was a mistake. More broadly, I think the idea that we ought to pray for the things we wish were true about us, even if we currently fall short, is a useful one.

And Lead Us Not Into Temptation

This is another bit where the Council of Trent makes some rather Christian assumptions - that "temptation" is something the Devil does and that is an unquestionable feature of existence. That being said, I think anyone who has been alive past childhood has experienced the draw of thoughts, beliefs, or behaviors that some part of them more readily dismisses. What I thought was most helpful in the Council of Trent's analysis was the notion that temptation is an intrinsic part of being alive, and so no amount of prayer will get you to a life "without temptation" - instead, the goal is no more temptation than you can handle with God's help. This seems both a) much more do-able, and b) more consistent with other theological interpretations. For example, Christian theology might have it that making love to a bunch of strangers is lustful and therefore bad. On the other hand, if you belong to a belief that has looser beliefs about sex, you might still believe that having sex with strangers might be bad if a) you've pledged yourself to someone else, or b) it gets in the way of forming a more serious relationship with one person. In this case, the "temptation" to have sex with multiple new folks might still be very strong, but the Christian and non-Christian beliefs about why it should be avoided would be different, even if they came up with the same recommendation.

Lastly, I appreciate the reminder that this part of the prayer is useful both for dealing with "avoiding temptation" in the abstract, or in general, and also in the moment to resist specific temptations. Whatever negative stuff you want to avoid, you should likely pray for daily, as well as whenever you find yourself needing help in the moment.

But Deliver Us From Evil

Much as "Thy Kingdom Come" holds all of the good you might ask for in any prayer, so "deliver us from evil" holds the wish to have everything not good taken away. In some sense, everything asked for above is held within this - as all of the good things asked for are, in fact, the lack of evil. One nice thing about this bit of the prayer is that folks tend to pray to get rid of what's wrong anyway, even if not especially religious ("no atheists in foxholes" and so forth). All that being said, this bit of the prayer is at the end, because asking for spiritual goods is more important overall. Also, some seeming "evils" might actually be good for our souls. Lastly, this says "from Evil", rather than "from evils", as Christianity sees all evil as coming from one source (the devil), and acknowledges that some material circumstances might seem evil, while actually being good for you - this way of praying allows for experiencing things that seem crappy in the moment, but might be for the best in the long run.

Amen

The prayer ends with a word rich with weight from scripture. Most importantly, "amen" works as a "seal" to mark the prayer as done, but with a feeling of gladness. In Catholic ceremonies, the congregation does not say "amen", but instead the priest says "amen" back to them, which they treat as a sign that the prayer has been heard and will be acted upon. According to the Council, even when praying alone, "amen" can work as a glad celebration of coming closer to God, a reminder of what the prayer just finished was truly for.

The Theology of the Prayer

Now, one thing I kept running into while reading the Council's analysis was that their arguments were cogent, coherent, and self-consistent - if you accept Christian theology. If your beliefs differ, it's a bit less helpful. Even so, I think that identifying the main strands of theological belief will be helpful in coming up with what others might get out of an analysis of the "Our Father".

First off, the prayer is assumed to be directed to the single source of all goodness, God. If you are coming from a non-monotheistic standpoint, you might see the value in having a single source of all goodness, but you might not attribute it to a single "God". Regardless of what this source of all goodness is, the prayer opens by addressing it joyfully, follows up by wishing for it to be realized in the world as much as possible, and then acknowledges that all day-to-day material goods also come from this source of all goodness, and closes by acknowledging that we all fall short of what we would like, and asking for help in doing the right thing.

Secondly, since this is, in fact, a Christian prayer, we have to deal with concept of original sin. Original sin is implicit in much of the prayer, and explored in more depth by the Council's analysis. The entire prayer basically assumes that the one praying is in a fallen state and needs redemption, and that this prayer is one of the chief tools in seeking and achieving that end. Obviously this comes out most clearly in the lines about forgiving trespasses, leading not into temptation, and delivering from evil. As I'll get into below, this strikes me as likely one of the biggest theological differences another faith would have to work around if looking for inspiration from this prayer. Related to this is the single source of all Evil mentioned above, which has a certain tidiness to it, but again, doesn't jive with everyone's view of the world.

Thirdly, much of the prayer expects those praying to do their share of the work, which I find quite interesting, and likely useful to apply outside of a Christian context. Though God is acknowledged as all powerful and the author of all good things, at least according to the Council of Trent, the one praying still has to do work and pray and believe and otherwise live consistently with what's being asked for, rather than just sitting back and waiting for God to handle everything. That might be the element of the Council's analysis that I found most interesting - it consistently stressed the active, engaged role folks need to take to get the most of prayer specifically and their spiritual life more broadly.

What Can We Learn for Other Faiths?

Despite the challenges of generalizing from a strongly Christian starting point, I think that there are some helpful lessons for crafting prayers for other faiths. Structurally speaking, I rather like the progression of the "Our Father" - as mentioned above, it begins by naming to Whom the prayer is addressed, asks for/affirms faith in the highest good things that Being can give, goes into more earthly requests, and closes with another universal, but this time "flipped" (deliver us from Evil rather than grant us the Kingdom of Heaven). So, I think that following the structure for prayers in other faiths would not present too much of a challenge, and could even be followed pretty closely, if wanted, by substituting in things appropriate to the Being prayed to. My Lord of Fallhall's Prayer is pretty close to that.

What gives a bit more of a challenge is looking at the prayer's theology and thinking through how to craft a prayer that fulfills a functional role similar to what the "Our Father" does for Christianity, even if it's actual structure is different - ideally, one that also functions as a "single, sufficient prayer" for a different system of belief. Drawing on the theological threads we stepped through above, the three core things we identified the prayer doing functionally are 1) acknowledging/celebrating the source of all Goodness, 2) seeking redemption as a sinful being in a fallen world, and 3) calling upon the one praying to show his faith and belief by living a life consistent with what is asked for in the prayer.

These are highly specific to the Christian faith, and if we want to write a prayer for another faith, we'll have to express some different things. So, I've taken the three threads above and generalized them further: 1) is either the highest good, its source, or both, 2) is whatever this faith most seeks, what "problem it solves", and 3) is an affirmation to live consistent with your beliefs. For example, for Druidry, 1) might be the concept Awen, 2) might be knowledge, power, and peace, and 3) might be asking to live in harmony with nature. This might be harder for religions that don't have something as clear cut that they seek as Christianity is about seeking forgiveness for sins, or a more multi-faceted concept about what the highest good is or where it comes from.

Altogether, I haven't worked out a prayer for my own beliefs following these recommendations yet, but I am glad I went through this exercise, I think it's been a helpful step, and I look forward to coming up with something.

Lessons Learned on Prayer from the Council of Trent

Date: 2022-August-05

After asking for some resources on prayer on a past Magic Monday, samchevre pointed me to The Catechism of the Council of Trent. Sure enough, despite its explicit Catholic Christian focus, I found a lot of good thinking on prayer as a practice, and I'd like to share what I thought were the most helpful takeaways for folks of any faith.

1. Kinds of Prayer

Asking for Things: Maybe the most common kind of prayer, the Council of Trent held that it was right and proper to ask God in prayer for anything it was right for folks to have. While it is certainly possible to ask for the wrong kind of things in prayer, and it takes some practice to ask for the right kind of things (more on both below), the evidence we have from just about every spiritual tradition that has recorded prayers shows folks asking the Gods for things that they want. If done right, this is not just a "gimme, gimme!" or a business transaction, but instead a way of acknowledging that you can't do anything in the world without your wider context and the help of the Holy.

Thanksgiving: Even if you are uncomfortable with asking for things in prayer, I think it's hard, if not impossible, to find anything wrong with prayers of thanksgiving. Even materialists acknowledge how worthwhile being consciously thankful is. Even more so than asking for things in prayer, giving thanks for what you have in this life is a good way to acknowledge that you are dependent on wider and higher things.

2. Outcomes of Prayer

Bringing You Closer to the Holy: I think the biggest thing prayer does for you is to bring you closer to the Holy and open you up to its influence. However you pray, it's a way to take some time to think about and reach out to whatever you think of as Holy, and hopefully to listen for some kind of answer. Many spiritual practices can do this, but prayer has the strengths of being ready to do whenever and wherever you are, and being changeable to whatever your needs are right now.

Acknowledging Something Greater than Yourself: I talked about this in both kinds of prayer above, but most any kind of prayer leads you to acknowledge that you need the wider world and its God(s). Even for things you did "by yourself", you still have your parents, teachers, employers, and culture to thank, to say nothing of any Gods or Goddesses. While humility can go too far into abasing yourself or groveling, most of us can use a timely reminder that we are not an island and that the world has given us a great many gifts that we ought to be thankful for.

Giving a Gift: Prayer can also be a gift to the Gods, the same as incense or a candle or mead. The more care and thought you put into the prayer, and the better it is said, the better it works as such. This is the most helpful when you lack the means to give something else, but is still worthwhile even if you have all of the offerings you might wish for.

Helping the Dead: Most faiths believe that the dead can use some help in the afterlife, and that prayer is one way of offering that help. For Christians, you pray for souls in Purgatory, other faiths might pray for souls to learn the lessons of their past life as smoothly as they might so they can move on to another life, and so forth. Whatever you believe about the afterlife (well, I guess, unless you don't believe in one), it seems that prayer is helpful to those in it.

3. How to Pray

Prayer Works Best as a Habit: The Catechism talks about prayer as a "duty", not something nice-to-have or optional. I don't know if that way of thinking is true for all faiths, but I think it is very much true that prayer works best as a habit, something you do at least every day. Much of the good of prayer only seems to come when you have done it for awhile with unalike things going on in your life. From what I have seen, it seems best to find many little ways to bring prayer into your life - something to say when you wake up, when you go to sleep, before you eat, when you put on a worn holy symbol, and so forth, as well as a "main" prayer set aside as its own thing. Of course, all of this doesn't have to be piled on at once, as that's not how good habit-building works, but over time, adding such things helps to make prayer a given, rather than something that takes will and forethought.

Pray in the Right Frame of Mind with the Right Feeling: For Christians, the Council of Trent recommends that Christians go into prayer with 1) utmost humilty, 2) sorrow or regret for sins done, 3) a lack of unworthy feelings like wrath or pride, and 4) faith in God. Once praying, the prayer should be heartfelt, with the ideal being that you "pour out" what is in your heart and soul with great warmth. I feel like the strong weight put on humility and sinfulness may not fit with every other faith, but the heart of the thought, that you should put yourself in a frame of mind in keeping with what is best to your Gods and then pray earnestly and with true feeling, seems like a good one to me.

Ask for the Right Things: The Catechism gave what struck me as a remarkably good rede about asking for things in prayer. First, get in the habit of asking God(s) for all things that you want in your life. Second, practice only asking for things that bring you closer to God(s). This is a really good way to build habits - you start by harnessing your natural yearning to have stuff to get you to pray often. Hopefully, as you do this, you start thinking about and acknowledging the role of the Holy in bringing good things into your life. Having gotten used to thinking about God(s) shaping what comes into your life, you can more readily think about what kinds of things God(s) might most want you to do in the world, and you can start asking for that. Through this change, you learn to ask for the right things, which makes even your prayers asking for stuff into a way to come closer to the Holy.

Beware Prayer Becoming a Way to Do Unworthy Things: For all the good that prayer can and does do, it can also become hollow, or even worse, truly harmful. If you only say the words or go through the motions, not only won't you get much out of prayer, you might sour on it or even on the Holy. There's some place for "fake it till you make it" in any kind of habit building, but if prayer feels hollow for a long time, it's likely not doing you any good, and you might need to work out a new way of doing things. Still worse is if you let prayer become a tool for your pride - showing off, making a big fuss about how strong your belief is, or the like. The Council refers to Jesus's well-known rede to pray indoors, out of the public eye. I think there's a role for public prayer, but a good rule of thumb seems to be that if you only pray when others are watching, you're likely doing it wrong.

A Lone Sufficient Prayer Can Be Helpful to Have: The Catechism puts forth the "Our Father" as a wholly sufficient prayer by itself. In other words, as a Christian, if you only pray the Our Father, you're doing everything you need to do with prayer. Other prayers are helpful and do a lot of good, of course, but they say that you only really need this one prayer. I found this a highly interesting thought. I think it would be very helpful to look at your faith and ask yourself "if I could only say one prayer for the rest of this life, and I wanted to get at everything that is most important in my faith, what would that prayer be?" I think that most faiths likely don't have a ready-to-go answer the way Christianity does (perhaps the "Bond Among Druids" for Revival Druidry), but that writing it would be a worthy goal to strive for.

Some Hands-On Tips: The Council also gave a handful of more hands-on tips that I thought were helpful: 1) "Mental Prayer" (praying without speaking out loud) not only works, but is often best suited to sharing strong, deep feelings. That being said, praying out loud helps you stay focused and can be a spur to feelings of devotion. 2) Food and drink can make it harder to have the right mindset or feeling for prayer, so it might be best to pray without having eaten or had drinks besides water for some time. Fasting can also bring on an even stronger feeling for prayer. 3) Try to make your deeds outside of prayer line up with what you ask for in prayer. If you ask for help, but give no help to others, you shouldn't think that will work out well for you.

Something Is Wrong - Beginning

Date: 2022-January-30

Most of the time, I'd rather write about what I'd like to see more of in the world, rather than what's wrong with it, but I worry that much of what I'd like to share won't mean much without setting up a few things. In short: something deep and widespread is wrong with the world. It's hard to spot if you go looking for it straight-on, but you catch glimpses of it here and there from the corners of your eyes.

It is the thrown-out plastic water bottle in an otherwise spotless stream. It is the streaming comedy that rings brittle and hollow. It is the buzzing of a notification for a social media app. It is the sneering look of cynical contempt that curdles a young face.

Call it ennui. Call it anomie. Call it nihilism. Call it by whichever French philosophical word you like. It is an Immoral Maze. It is Uruk. It is Moloch.

It is all around us, and most of the ways put forward to deal with it are wholly lacking.

So what might we do about this, whatever it is? I hope that pinning it down a bit might give us a few ideas of what is to be done, and then we can go onto the more welcome work of fleshing those out.

Destinations and Journeys

Date: 2022-January-22

The other day, I read this post the other day by an online acquaintance, which reinforced the old-but-good maxim that it is not the destination that matters, but rather the journey. I agree with this, but in responding to the post, I had a few thoughts on it that I thought I'd get down and post here.

The Destination is not the Goal

So, you've likely heard some variation of this fortune-cookie-style wisdom before, that the journey is what matters, not where you're going. As I said above, I think this is mostly true and worth keeping in mind, since our psychology and society often push us into laser-focusing on the destination with remarkable tunnel vision. It's helpful to be reminded that "life moves pretty fast - if you don't stop and look around once in a while, you might miss it." Also, every single successful person you ever talk to will tell you that the experience of achieving what they were after was nowhere near as engaging and interesting as the pursuit. Also, paradoxically, if you start out overly focused on outcomes and end points, you might just miss out on developing what you need to develop to even reach them at all. Often, focusing on the process instead of the goal is how you get to the goal at all. But notice in all of these examples, we're still talking about goals and destinations, they're still assumed.

Destinations are Excuses for Journeys

Okay, so journeys = good and destinations = bad, right? We should just stop setting goals or having specific places to go, right? Well, maybe not so much. There's a difference between a journey and wandering - the destination. So, we have a bit of a paradox. We want to value destinations less than journeys, but we need destinations to have journeys. What I realized in responding to my friend's post is that destinations are excuses for journeys - necessary, but not sufficient, conditions for what we're looking for. I keep having this insight over and over again playing with my daughter - I'll get frustrated that she's being slow to take her turn on Candy Land, or that she gets distracted by a stick while kicking her soccer ball into a goal. On my better days, though, I'll catch myself and realize "oh, right, achieving the 'goal' of this activity isn't actually why we're doing this, it's so that she can have fun and spend time with me and learn things". Another helpful example for me is playing games. Within the game itself, sure, you're playing to win, but you're really playing the game to have fun with your friends, so you likely shouldn't act like a dick to try to win. So how do we recognize that we need destinations so that we'll take journeys, while not attaching too much importance to them?

How Do you Balance these?

I like to think about it as something similar to the advice to have "strong opinions, loosely held" that you'll run into in the startup world. It's helpful to have specific goals or destinations, maybe even ambitious ones, but not to focus on them so hard that you lose sight of what's around you on the way. One thing I think helps do this is to be suspicious of "efficiency", when that means "get to the outcome as fast as possible". Sure, sometimes efficiency is worthwhile, but only when it is compatible with whatever other goals or values the journey might be meant to support. If you purposely chose to take the scenic route for the sights and stops, it's foolish to speed once you get on it. If you asked your kid to help clean up so she learns to be helpful and responsible, then it's dumb to do things for her because she's slow about it. If you started a company to have freedom and autonomy, it's foolish to take a deal that puts you in someone else's pocket. So, to properly value the journey, we have to think about not where we're going, but why we chose that end point in the first place, and be ready to adjust along the way.

Reading Goal for 2022

Date: 2022-January-05

This year, I've set myself a reading goal inspired by John Michael Greer who describes his reading habits as "mostly reading books by dead people". Since I've been reading a lot of his books recently and that approach jives with some of my other thoughts on reading, I decided to set myself the challenge for 2022 of literally following that advice, so here's the rule I've set for myself:

For all of 2022, I will read only books written by people who have died, with the following exceptions: 1. I need to read it for work, 2. I am using it to learn a craft I actually put into practice during 2022, or 3. it is by a close friend or family member.

I have already bent the rules slightly, as I finished reading a novel by the afore-mentioned JMG on the 2nd that I started after Christmas. From here on out, though, I plan to follow the above rule very strictly. So why am I doing this?

A few reasons. First, I have been looking for more ways to follow the Murakami's advice to not read what other people are reading, and older books are a good way to do that. "The author is dead" makes for a decent, if somewhat dramatic, proxy for "this book is older". Secondly, it also works as a decent proxy for the "Lindy Effect" - the notion that the longer a work has been readily available, the more likely it is to be something worthwhile. Thirdly, I hope that it will help me rein in my natural tendency to go haring off after whatever is new and shiny, and instead finally get around to reading some stuff that's been on my list for a while, especially some classic works.

So, there we are. Right now, I'm about a third of the way through God is Red by Vine Deloria, Jr., and I think next up I'll go for the provocatively titled Decline of the West by Oswald Spengler. I'll keep you posted on how it goes!

Further Thoughts for Students

Date: 2021-December-28

For now, I'm lucky enough to get to share my thoughts with students every semester. That being said, there's a lot of stuff that I think is really helpful that I can't justify sharing in my course, as it's not so much about "business" or "communication". So, I reckoned I'd gather up that kind of thing here. Mostly, it's stuff I wish I had known when I was younger, so it ought to be very helpful to young folks. On the other hand, I wasn't all that young when I learned a lot of this, so you might still find something helpful here even if you're not so young.

Given that this is the internet, and that I teach at a university, I feel like I have to say clearly that these are my own views, and not those of my employer. I've posted them here to help maintain some of that distance. If you see anything here that upsets you, I'd like to hear from you so we can talk it over, especially if you're one of my students.

Your Head

Let's start with some things that I have found very helpful in learning how to think.

Learn and Use Mental Models

My good friend Jonathan introduced me to "mental models", which are perhaps best explained by the blog Farnam Street, but here's the short version: different fields have their own ways of thinking about the world, and many of these ways can be applied outside of their home field. Naming these ways of thinking as more-or-less contained "models" makes it easier to remember and apply them in a variety of situations. The other reason it's helpful to think of them as separate "models" is that it reminds you that they are just that: useful abstractions that help you understand the world, not the raw, objective truth.

Learn How to Read a Book

Anyone who wants to read books for more than fun needs to read How to Read a Book by Adler and Van Doren. If you haven't read it, your education has a serious gap. They break down reading into four levels: 1) Elementary, 2) Inspectional, 3) Analytical, 4) Syntopic. 1) Elementary reading is what you learned in grade school: marks on a page form meaningful words - that's all most folks know how to do. 2) Inspectional reading could be called "intelligent skimming" - you shoot to get the most out of a book in a fixed amount of time. 3) Analytical reading is where things get interesting. This is when you want to get the most out of a book, period. You take all the time you need to grok it in fullness. 4) Syntopic reading is when you want to understand a concept or field of study and so you read a lot of works by different authors and make sense of them all together. Even if you don't follow the exact scheme of this book or the techniques it outlines, I think it is very useful to have an idea of different levels of reading depending on what you're trying to do, and to apply them intentionally.

Use Divergence and Convergence

I was introduced to this pair of concepts by my former boss Tony, who learned them by working with Dr. Min Basadur and becoming certified in his creative problem solving approach (which is currently called "Simplexity"). In creative thinking, you have two processes which are both necessary, but are better done separately: "diverging" and "converging". Diverging is where you try to generate as many options as possible. Convergence is when you evaluate the options generated and try to pick the best ones based on whatever criteria is relevant. These two ways of thinking use very different mental processes, and they interfere with each other. This approach applies in a surprisingly broad number of fields: in writing, you want to write a crappy first draft and edit, if you're figuring out where to go to dinner with your friends, throw out any options you can think of without worrying about if any of them are 'good', and only then pick something from that list. So, any time you're trying to solve a problem, first come up with as many options as you can, pause, and then look at those options and ask which ones work. Repeat as needed.

Seek Out Old, Weird Things

Don't waste your time trying to fit in. Not because fitting in isn't important - it is. But that shit will take care of itself. Even the weirdest, most against-the-grain bastard you can think of is still a product of his time and culture. So, unless you are already a hermit living deep in the woods, cultivate an interest in the obscure, the far off, and the long ago. Even though there are certain charms to eccentricity in and of itself, that's not what I'm pushing here. In a big, highly-linked world, your best hope for being valuable is to bring a combination of talents and knowledge to the table that no one else has, not by doing what everyone around you is doing. The two best heuristics I know of for making this happen are to go after what is weird and what is old. When you start poking around weird/old things, you're going to run into a lot of mularkey, but this is actually a feature, not a bug - you get to practice evaluating sources and how they fit in with what else you know. If everything you encounter fits into a neat, coherent system without this work, you are missing or distorting large chunks of reality, because reality is messy. What do I mean by "weird"? Anything that would get a snooty look at a cocktail party is a good start - cryptids, UFOs, alternate versions of history, magic, conspiracies, and so forth. Am I saying you should believe all of this stuff? Of course not. The idea is to expose yourself to ways of thinking that are very different from what you'll usually encounter - sometimes, when used carefully, some of those ways of thinking are useful. And that leaves "old", this one is simpler - if it was written before you were born, chances are pretty good that it contains some ways of looking at the world that seem pretty alien to you, which again, is the point of the exercise.

Learn and Recognize the Three Economies

I encountered this model in John Michael Greer's The Wealth of Nature: Economics as if Survival Mattered, which itself draws heavily on the work of E.F. Schumacher. The book was helpful for a variety of reasons, but the best bit was a mental model (see above). Greer breaks down "the economy" into three separate economies that work very differently: the Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Economies. I'll start in the middle with the most familiar: the Secondary Economy is the economy of goods and services. I cut up meat to sell to the baker and use the proceeds to buy the products of the candlestick maker. Economics as a field is very good at analyzing the Secondary Economy, but tends not to think much about the Primary Economy, which is everything the natural world does to allow human economic activity to happen at all. When a farmer buys seed and a tractor and works his tuchus off cultivating and harvesting his crops, that's all Secondary Economy work. The fact that plants evolved little starch pods called grain, that the microbes in the soil allow plants to grow in it, that bees pollinate the plants, and that the sun shines down free energy for photosynthesis is all the Primary Economy. Lastly, we have the Tertiary Economy, which is the economy of financial instruments like money, loans, and stocks. Theoretically, everything in the Tertiary Economy is a representation of something in the Primary or Secondary Economy: I have a piece of paper called a "Title" that says I own a chunk of land, but the bank has a piece of paper saying they get the land if I don't pay back the loan they gave me to buy the title, and so on. So long as the Tertiary Economy remains pretty lightly linked with the Primary and Secondary economies, it's incredibly useful - using money to settle transactions and measure wealth has lots of benefits, and other financial instruments help to start bigger, more complex businesses that can be valuable to everybody. Unfortunately, the Tertiary Economy has a tendency to become unmoored from underlying economic reality and to eat up more and more of the total share of economic activity in the form of speculative bubbles. The more emphasis on the Tertiary Economy, the more bubbles like this happen. Our current economy is something like 75% in the Tertiary by notional dollar value, which is definitely something to think about.

Your Heart

Next, some touchier-feelier stuff about what matters in life.

Work Out Your Relationship to Meaning

This is the bit of this post I had the hardest time writing. It's big, weighty, and feelings run very strong around it. So I'll just try to dive in. At one time or another, all of us grapple with questions like "what's the point of all this?" or "how do I know what to really care about?" Some of us find answers that work for us in the teachings of a religion or a philosophy. Others of us look to what science and reason can tell us. And some of us try to bring our eyes down to the here and now and focus on what's right in front of us and set aside such big, maybe unanswerable questions. Perhaps the most common response is to distract ourselves from the questions so that we don't have to think about them. Television, booze, sex, jobs, gadgets, jewelry - whatever our own vice might be, we find something that lets us ignore those questions for a while. This is a bad long-term strategy, for at some point, something will happen that will show you the distractions for what they are - empty, meaningless, a waste of your precious time and attention. Sometimes this can hit like a ton of bricks: maybe someone you love dies, or maybe the relationship you've built your life around ends, or you find out you've only got a few months to live. If you wait for these heavy moments to think seriously about meaning, you're gonna have a bad time. Instead, start now. I won't tell you what traditions to explore, and I certainly won't tell you what conclusions to come to. This kind of thing is irreducibly personal, and what works for me is unlikely to work for you. If you're at a loss on where to start, there's an online book called Meaningness that I think does a pretty good job of outlining many of the reasons this is a thorny problem, approaches that have historically been tried and some of the problems with them, and so forth. You could also do worse than to read some ancient philosophy. If there's a religious tradition that's familiar or appeals to you, you might see what it has to offer. Wherever you start, don't expect this to be a quick project, something you sort out over the summer and never think about again. Wrestling with meaning is a life-long challenge, but one that can make everything else far more worthwhile.

Meditate Discursively

Lots of folks these days take seriously the idea of taking a bit of time every day to get your mind right, which is great. On the other hand, almost all of them practice forms of "mind-emptying" techniques that seek to still conscious thought, which is a bit less great. Don't get me wrong, there's nothing wrong with such techniques in and of themselves, but they have some little talked-about downsides. First off, very many folks find them tough to get started with or keep up. Secondly, even for folks who make a habit of it, many don't get that much out of them. Thirdly, and most seriously, a small number of people have very strong negative psychological reactions (most often if they jump straight into something like a week-long silent retreat with no prior training). Luckily, there's another method of meditation called "Discursive Meditation", which I learned from John Michael Greer. This method has a long history, tremendous benefits, and pretty much perfect safety.

Here's how to do it: sit up straight in a chair, breathe deeply, and spend several minutes thinking about a specific theme of spiritual/philosophical significance. That's it! If you'd like more detail, check out the link above for a series of posts that lay it out step by step. You get all the good of training your attention not to wander, breathing deeply, and paying attention to your posture, while also giving your thinking mind a good workout as well. I've tried a wide variety of meditation techniques, and this one has been by far the most rewarding for me. Your mileage may, of course, vary, but think over giving it a shot. It's easy, safe, and works equally well whatever your spiritual beliefs (or lack thereof) may be.

Express Romantic Interest First, Friendliness Later

Given the expectations and norms of our current culture, this one might be most useful for heterosexual males, but I hope it's helpful to whoever might need it. Many young folks, when they find themselves romantically interested in someone take an approach of "let's get to be friends, and then I'll ask her/him out later". This sounds reasonable, but is actually a terrible plan born of cowardice. Yes, I know, harsh, but that harshness comes from my own bitter experience of trying to follow this plan back in the day. Basically, asking someone out is scary, and we scramble to mitigate that fear any way we can. It seems like asking someone out will be less scary if we know the other person already likes us as a friend. Plausible, but crap. Being friends is not the same as being lovers, they're different relationships with different dynamics and different needs. Of course, there is some overlap, but it's actually easier to go from "we went on a date and there wasn't any chemistry" to "so let's just be friends" than it is to go from "we feel comfortable together as friends" to "so do you want to risk ruining that with awkwardness by going on a date?" So, if there's someone you think you might want to date, ask them out instead of trying to be friends first.

Have Kids If You Want Them

So yes, kids aren't for everyone, and you definitely shouldn't have a kid if you aren't ready or don't really want one, but overall, kids are pretty great. I wanted to state this explicitly, because from the outside, it's easy to see what sucks about having kids: they take your time, they're expensive, they throw fits, they cover you in snot and get you sick, and so on. The thing is, from the outside, it's also harder to see what is great about having kids: watching a human gradually come online, seeing your own facial expressions mirrored back to you, having someone who feels emotions more intensely than you even know how to run up, throw their arms around you, and bury their face in your shoulder. There is absolutely nothing like it.

Learn from the Stoics

There's a lot of great stuff in ancient philosophy, but if I had to pick one thing that's most helpful, it would be the core insight of Stoicism: "you can't control what happens to you, but you can control how you react to it". Simple to state, hard to live. Someone cuts you off in traffic? You didn't make that happen, but you have full control over whether you get mad, how mad you get, and what you do because you're mad. Again, I know, easier said that done. And the Stoics acknowledged that you might not be able to stop that initial flare of anger from appearing, but you are not your emotions. They are something that happen to you, and you can choose to engage with them or distance yourself from them. Sometimes anger is useful, because it motivates you to right wrongs. Other times it just stresses you out for no reason. Learning to get inside your own cognitive processes like this is enormously helpful. Instead of being jerked around by whatever emotion happens to flare up due to circumstances, you can decide to steer your life based on whatever values you find truly important.

Turn Off Notifications, Feeds, and News

Most folks say that time is your most valuable resource, but I'd tweak that to say that attention is your most valuable resource. We've all had hours or days or weeks where we didn't do much and felt vaguely crappy afterwards. We've also all had experiences of intense, focused attention, maybe only for a few minutes, that have stuck with us as peak moments. Time may be the quantitative measure of your finitude in this existence, but attention is the qualitative. Where I'm going with all this high-flying philosophy is that you should be utterly ruthless in guarding your attention from anyone and anything that tries to grab it without your express, willed intent. Ever had a pleasant evening spoiled when you got an text from someone wanting you to deal with some problem for them? Ever been right in the middle of straightening out a complicated argument in your head when an email alert has popped up and you lose that almost-there insight? Ever been at dinner with a date, you're really getting into the conversation, and then they check their phone? Notifications destroy the quality of whatever they interrupt, even if you don't do anything else with them right away. It's even worse when the notifications try to further hijack your attention - taking you to a social media post that is at the top of a rabbit hole you go down for hours or to a news story that freaks you out and ruins your mood for a whole weekend. Notifications are evil. Turn as many of them off as you can possibly get away with. Put your attention where you want it to go, and keep it there for as long as you decide.

Spend Some Time Outside

This one really is this simple: every day if you can, but at least once a week, go spend some time sitting or walking outdoors. If you can get somewhere with plants and animals, go there. If not, consider moving somewhere less hellish, but in the meantime, sun or moon and sky will do just fine. Most of us spend most of our lives in large boxes looking at smaller, brighter boxes, focusing on an abstract world of words, images, and thoughts. As such, we have very little connection to the actual, physical world that keeps us alive. So, go outside. This shouldn't be some doleful chore (though, on some days in the summer in Texas, it can feel that way). Go feel the warm sun on your face, watch squirrels get frisky, check out good-looking guys or gals. Fifteen or twenty minutes is great if you can get it, but even just a handful of minutes can do wonders.

Your Hands

Lastly, some practical stuff about what to do with and for your body.

Don't "Follow Your Passion"

This is the core idea in Cal Newport's So Good They Can't Ignore You. "Follow your passion" was a rallying cry for Baby Boomers who had followed the overly-conservative career advice of parents who lived through the Great Depression, and it was useful for getting folks used to the idea that maybe you shouldn't be miserable at your job if you can help it. Unfortunately, it was going too far to suggest that everyone has some deep, inborn passion that they just need to discover and their whole life will fall into place. Sure, some folks have these life-defining passions, but most of them figure that out pretty young. You ever met someone who can't help but draw, no matter what? That guy's got a passion, he doesn't need the advice to find it. Most of us don't have that. Instead, most of us become passionate about whatever we get good at. So, Newport recommends you find some skills that are both rare and valuable and then get good at them. Rare so that you're not a dime a dozen, and valuable so that other people will actually pay for it. Some examples: crafting ships in bottles is rare, but not so valuable. Being a decent, but not great, computer programmer is fairly valuable, but not that rare. So, if you want a challenging, meaningful career, find something hard to get good at that's in demand, and then get good at that. Once you do, you'll have more control over what you do, who you work for, and what you achieve.

Learn a Craft

Learn how to make something with your hands - scarves, bread, motorcycle engines, whatever grabs you. If you don't know what you might like making, try a wide variety of crafts (remember Divergence and Convergence?). Pay attention to what seems interesting even when you're not good at it (for me, that's woodcarving). Then take some classes, watch some videos, find a club, or whatever works - learn the skills you need and then make the time to do it. Getting really good might take a lot of work, but you can get to "just fine" in many things with very little practice: for example, I bet you could go from "bread comes from a shelf in the store" to baking tasty loaves in 2 or 3 tries, tops. The goal here is not to become an artisan and quit your job to do glassblowing full time (though, if you can make that work, more power to you). The point is to concentrate deeply and engage with something outside of yourself in the real world. You'll find there's a big difference between imagining what it takes to turn water, flour, and yeast into dough versus feeling a sticky substance and smooshing it around until it feels right. Not only is it helpful to ground yourself like this, it is hugely enjoyable! If you've never "lost yourself" in something you cared about, then you are truly missing out. Of the most meaningful things in my life, almost all of them involved this kind of focused attention.

Make the Right Thing Easier

I learned this from the book The House that Cleans Itself by Mindy Starns Clark. If you have tried something multiple times with the best of intentions, but for some reason it won't stick, "I'll do it again, but this time I'll just try harder" is most likely a losing strategy. Instead, find a way to make it easier to do the right thing. For example, mail used to pile up on the credenza by the door. So, my wife and I put a two-drawer inbox on the credenza and a recycling bin next to it. Now, it's easy to sort what's mine or hers, and any junk mail goes right in the recycling. Apparently, it was too hard to walk the 15 feet to the main recycling bin, but it's not too hard to drop it within arm's reach. Go figure. Seriously, sometimes the tweaks that make something doable will be downright embarrassing. You can apply this concept in lots of areas of your life: set up automatic savings, don't have junk food in the house, put your alarm where you have to get out of bed to turn it off, have charging cables everywhere you might need to charge your phone, and so forth. There are some things where the answer is training your willpower, but more often than you might think, you can find a way to make it easier to win.

Fake It Till You Make It

This advice has become much more widespread of late, but allow me to reiterate: you will not be good at something when you start. You get good at it by doing it. Humans are imitative animals, so if you want to be good at something, act as much like someone who is good at it as you can, and then keep at it. Eventually, the act will become the reality (likely far before you believe it). Don't put anybody at risk, of course, but it is almost always much safer to risk failing than you're afraid it is.

Drink Water

If you're anything like me, you might have grown up drinking too much other liquid (like Dr. Pepper) and not enough water. Luckily, the Army beat this out of me, so I now take hydration very seriously. If you don't drink much water, you will be shocked at how much better you feel if you get enough. Here's how to do it: buy a 1 quart bottle that you will find convenient to carry around pretty much everywhere. Set a goal of drinking 2 of these a day (more if you do something that makes you sweat much). Set specific times/events by which to empty the bottle - for example "I'll drink one by lunch time and the next one by the end of the work day". If you find yourself getting light-headed, add a tiny pinch of salt to one or both bottles. If you are thirsty, add a 3rd or 4th bottle. If you drink any alcohol, consider going for that 3rd or 4th bottle just in case, and make sure you get some salt either in food or added to your water. Seriously, if you haven't been getting enough water, you have no idea how much it helps: headaches, tiredness, muscle growth, weight loss - all better with water.

Stretch

Most of us far more time sitting than our bodies are built for (see the point about How to Stand/Sit), which leaves us stiff. So get some stretching in your life. You can experiment, but here are a few good options to try out: Sun Salutations, the Five Rites, Daily Dozen , Gymnastic Strength Training Daily Limber. Whatever you pick, pay special attention to your back, since that's where a lot of the bad effects of sitting show up, but it's all linked up, so find a whole body routine. If you have a more intensive stretching practice, like Yoga or Gymnastic training or the like, then that's awesome, but even if you are otherwise a couch potato, do yourself a favor and spend a few minutes stretching every day.

Stand (and Sit) Well

Okay, this one is best done by demonstration, but it's important enough that I didn't want to leave it out here. As folks who spend far too much time sitting, we are all bad at standing, which paradoxically also makes us bad at sitting. We stand and sit in ways that damage our skeletons and circulatory systems, so it'd be great to fix that. If you want to go real deep, check out Deskbound: Standing Up to a Sitting World by Dr. Kelly Starrett, which is where I got this checklist for proper standing:

  1. Feet: Plant your feet, toes straight forward (not out to the side or pointed inward) about hip-width apart.
  2. Knees: Very slightly tense your muscles as if trying to turn your toes outward while keeping your heels planted, but don't let your toes actually move. You should feel this as a slight tension in your knees, which this movement stabilizes.
  3. Hips: Tense your butt cheeks to do a humping motion with your hips, moving your tail bone down and forward. For a less crude metaphor, imagine that your hips are a cup and that you don't want to pour any liquid out the front. This stabilizes your hips.
  4. Lower Back: Gently tense your abs, about 1/3 of full tension, pulling them back toward your spine
  5. Upper Back: Rotate your arms in your shoulder sockets. To do this, with your hands at your sides, rotate them until your thumbs face forward.
  6. Head: Move your head a bit forward and backward and feel for the spot where it's sitting right on top of the stable pillar you just made from the ground up.

Congratulations, you are now standing the way your body was designed for! Keeping your body in this position every waking hour is a bit much, but I use this process as a handy check-in to "reset" when I notice myself slouching.

To sit, just do steps 4-6, but you'll notice it's much harder. Your legs, and especially your hips, are doing a lot of the work of maintaining good posture when you stand, and when you sit, you basically don't have them to help.

Storytelling Part 5: Resources for Digging Deeper

Date: 2017-November-27

I’ve done my best to share a fantastic beginner’s guide to storytelling with this series, but there is a lot of depth to all of the topics that I’ve touched on. Below, I provide my recommendations on resources for digging into Story Structure, Effective Practice, Public Speaking, Oral Storytelling, Writing, and Visual Storytelling. In each section, I’ve provided a handful of my favorite “how to” resources, whether books, blogs, videos, or courses along with a section of illustrative examples applying the lessons you’ll learn from the how to books. These lists are by no means exhaustive, and if I’ve missed any of your favorite works on storytelling, please let me know, as I am always hungry for new insights on the topic.

Story Structure

How to Do It Yourself

The Story Grid by Shawn Coyne

If you want to dive deep into structure and how to put together a story that works, you could do a lot worse than to read The Story Grid. Shawn Coyne takes an editor’s approach to figuring out the nuts and bolts of what “works” in a story or not. The book focuses mostly on fiction, but he has regular blog posts and further material at the website that apply it to all sorts of other examples.

The Hero with a Thousand Faces by Joseph Campbell

I dare you to time how long you can read about story structure without Joseph Campbell coming up. This book is where he lays out the fundamental structure shared by almost every enduring mythological tale across the world. Something about this sort of story speaks to the fundamental human experience, and has been for at least thousands of years. If you’ve ever heard of a “hero’s journey” or the “monomyth”, this book is where that comes from.

Save the Cat by Blake Snyder

Blake Snyder devotes his book to the end-to-end process of writing a screenplay, and so contains a lot of material not directly related to structure, but it has a rock-solid basic approach to thinking about structure, and even better, hands-on details about how to actually do that. My personal favorite thing from the book is “The Board” - a board you put up on the wall that shows the breakdown into acts, on which you place index cards for each scene, and for each scene you track the change in emotional charge and what the conflict is. The Story Grid covers a lot of the same ground, but they are useful to compare and cherry pick from.

On the Shapes of Stories by Kurt Vonnegut

This delightful (and short) video shares both Vonnegut’s clear understanding of story structure as well as his wit. I recommend watching it a few times: the first time to enjoy it as an entertaining presentation, and then again to realize “oh wait, there’s actually something there.”

See Others Do It Well

Mythology by Edith Hamilton

If you want to see the basic myths that Campbell was talking about, there are fewer better presentations than Edith Hamilton’s collection of Greek myths. Academically rigorous but easy to read, this is the “one book” on classical mythology.

Tootsie

Screenwriting classes love to use Tootsie as an example, because it’s just a good, solid story with a structure that works. Every scene moves the story forward, every scene has a shift in emotional charge, every scene has conflict, and the character grows exactly the way we want to see him do so.

A Christmas Story

I am especially tickled to point to this example because it is framed as a narrator telling a story - aren’t we all so meta. Every anecdote in the seemingly scattered remembrances of the narrator actually contributes to the overall story, following that Red Rider BB gun. It’s extremely tight and on point the whole time, which helps to explain why people happily re-watch every year.

Pulp Fiction

Another favorite of film nerds, Pulp Fiction serves as a great example of how structure does not equal chronology. Tartantino chooses to show us scenes based on what he wants us to know about the character at the time, which means that he can’t rely on normal causality to carry the plot along if he gets the sequencing wrong. We’re left with a movie where we buy into the plight of a lot of pretty bad dudes and enjoy struggling along with them.

The Dresden Files by Jim Butcher

Okay, this one is by the far the nerdiest thing I’m recommending, but hear me out. Each one of these novels works as a tightly structured hard boiled detective story - all the obligatory scenes and genre conventions are there in ways that are both satisfyingly familiar and intriguingly new. At the same time, each book builds the overall character arc of its titular protagonist, Harry Dresden (spoiler alert) and calls back to previous novels in both expected and unexpected ways. The quality certainly improves over the course of the series, but even the first few books are enjoyable stand alone detective stories.

Effective Practice

How to Do It Yourself

The 4-Hour Chef by Tim Ferriss

What does cooking have to do with Storytelling? Not much, but the first section of this book breaks down Tim’s recipe for deconstructing a new skill to learn and how to maximize the value of your practice. I like to think I’ve done a lot of the deconstructing and sequencing work for you on Storytelling with this series, but it’s a meta-skill worth learning. As an added bonus, if you don’t already know how to cook, this is the best learn-to-cook cookbook ever written.

The Little Book of Talent by Daniel Coyle

Short and to the point, Coyle distills the lessons he has learned from neuroscientists, coaches, and performers for building world-class talent into short tips. The book is helpfully organized into sections focused on getting started, building skills effectively, and maintaining motivation when it gets hard. Intended as a quick reference to be carried around and looked at again and again, this book won’t convince you that deliberate practice is the way to get great if you don’t already believe it, but it will help you immensely in actually doing the work.

The Art of Learning by Josh Waitzkin

Josh Waitzkin is terrifyingly brilliant and has an amazing ability to learn new fields to highest levels of mastery. His book is one of my personal operating manuals, and every time I return to it, I realize that I didn’t understand it as well as I thought before. His section on “Making Smaller Circles” is especially relevant for how to approach practice once you’ve already identified the skill or had the basic insight you need to work on.

Scott Young’s Blog

Scott is a fantastic resource on all things learning related, and he relentlessly looks for ways to make his learning more effective. You’ll see him come up again below in the examples section, but there’s an absolute wealth of free material on his blog. I especially recommend his advice on setting up your own ultralearning project if you want to really improve your storytelling.

Rapid Learner Course

Speaking of Scott Young, he has very thoughtfully put together all of his best material and experience on effectively learning things as quickly as possible into this online course. I have found it extremely effective in learning challenging material quickly on everything from languages through improving my coaching skills.

See Others Do It Well

Moonwalking with Einstein by Joshua Foer

While there are lots of works on improving your memory and even on memory competition, I chose to highlight Joshua Foer’s because it focuses on his own journey going from having no idea such as a thing as “mental athletics” existed to himself competing and succeeding as one. It provides a really impressive example of what you can achieve by learning the right way to practice and following through.

Scott Young’s Ultralearning Projects

If you aren’t sure that Scott Young knows what he’s talking about when it comes to learning things well quickly, check out his ultralearning projects. This dude completed the equivalent of an MIT undergrad in Computer Science in one year. For the most visually impressive example, check out his drawing project.

The King’s Speech

Dramatized, of course, but the true story of someone with some pretty serious challenges to telling stories working his butt off to get it right. Most of us don’t have quite as high stakes as King George VI declaring war on Germany, but we all have challenges to overcome and opportunities to tell an important story.

Presentations and Public Speaking

How to Do It Yourself

Show and Tell by Dan Roam

We’ve talked about this book before, but it is the best “one stop shop” on how to go from zero to giving great presentations I’ve seen. It discusses, structure, practice, and delivery in ways that will be familiar to you from this series, and has a great recommended reading list at the end, with some of the same titles you’ll see here. I especially appreciate its emphasis on tying together the spoken message and whatever visuals you are presenting, and how they can reinforce each other to create a stronger whole.

Dale Carnegie Course

There are a lot of different courses offered by Dale Carnegie Training these days, some focused more specifically on public speaking than others, but the underlying principle for all of them is to better understand and empathize with other people as human beings. I have not taken one of their courses yet, but I’ve heard nothing but good things and its on my list.

Toastmasters

Another recommendation that I have to provide on the strength of what I’ve heard, this is the best known way to make sure you get a lot more chances to speak in public and learn by doing. They have clubs all over the world and create a supportive environment for people to build their skills as a speaker and get meaningful feedback. If you don’t have a job that requires a lot of presentations (or if you do and are hungry for even more), this is a great way to get your reps in.

Mindtools Article

This article provides a nice overview of how to approach improving your public speaking, and goes into more specifics than we’ve covered in this general series on storytelling. The thing I like the most about it are the links - each section provides a basic overview, but there are a lot of links to dive deeper on specific topics as needed. That’s not all - the article linked is part of a series on giving presentations, which itself is part of a series on Communication skills. Mindtools has a pretty smart nesting and linking of related skills and makes a great resource for a motivated learner.

Your body language may shape who you are - Amy Cuddy

Otherwise known as “Fake it ’til you become it”, this is one of my favorite TED talks of all time, and is applicable to much more than just public speaking. It talks about the feedback loop created by our body language, the mental states they create, and what we believe about ourselves. Does public speaking terrify you? If so, the cure is to adopt the posture and body language of someone triumphant and powerful to counteract your natural scared body language. It sounds nuts, but it works.

See Others Do It Well

"Why we do what we do” by Tony Robbins

Tony Robbins is one of the most dynamic and engaging public speakers I’ve ever seen. If you want to see even more of him doing his thing, check out his outstanding Netflix documentary I Am Not Your Guru.

“Your elusive creative genius” by Elizabeth Gilbert

I snuck this one in for some bonus storytelling advice along with watching someone deliver an effective speech. Gilbert talks about the psychology necessary to do the actual work of writing (or anything else creative). Pairs extremely well with The War of Art.

"This is Water" by David Foster Wallace

Not quite as energetic or dynamic as the previous two, pay attention as you listen to this one to the more writerly approach Wallace takes with his structure, self-referential commentary, and asking the audience to get a bit meta.

“We Shall Fight Them on the Beaches” by Winston Churchill

One of the most famous speeches of all time. Notice how different the cadence and emphasis were than a modern politician would use. This guy knew he had your attention for the next 12 minutes, and he made sure to convey the gravitas the occasion demanded. I wouldn’t recommend that you try to copy Churchill’s style today, but take a look at the differences in assumptions about delivery and style between then and now, and ask yourself if you really want to stick with yours.

Oral Storytelling

How to Do It Yourself

The Art of Storytelling: From Parents to Professionals

This course focuses on the art of standing in front of people and telling them a story with your voice and goes into a lot of great nuts and bolts details. It also includes material on things like structure and characterization and so on, but what it is has that you can’t get from other sources is a lot of specific advice that doesn’t apply to writing or movie making or drawing comics and may or may not apply to large audience public speaking.

How to Talk to Anybody

Another course on general communication and social skills, this includes some very practical and easy to follow advice on telling stories, because stories are an essential part of good interpersonal skills. My favorite piece of advice: keep a story “toolbox” of ready-to-go stories you’ve practiced and tweaked and pull them out whenever it’s appropriate. Sounds obvious, but have you actually done it?

Storytelling: Art and Technique by Ellin Greene and Janice M. Del Negro

A classic textbook on oral storytelling as practiced in America. I have not read this yet, but it seems to be the definitive reference work on the subject and is cited all over the place.

Second City Improv Program

The famous Chicago-based group invented comedy improv as we know it, and has codified how to succeed when improvising. If you don’t happen to live in Chicago, there are improv groups just about everywhere. What does improv have to do with storytelling? It gets you used to finding relevant connections with other people and spinning them into a story, and also teaches you to read what’s working for your audience and what’s not. Plus, you’ll bomb a few times, which feels terrible, but then you keep going and you realize it’s not a big deal, which is crucial to build the confidence to tell entertaining stories with enough verve.

See Others Do It Well

Mike Birbiglia - What I Should Have Said Was Nothing

My friend recently pointed me in Birbiglia’s direction as a great example of a comedian who builds his humor through storytelling rather than relying as much on specific jokes. Patton Oswalt is another favorite of mine. I especially like to pay attention to what stand up comedians do because the most common reason we’re telling a story in our personal lives is to entertain people and make them laugh - so who better to check out than people that get paid to do it?

Hardcore History by Dan Carlin

Podcasts are another favorite form of verbal storytelling for me, because they have to be good enough to work without visuals of any kind. Carlin does a remarkable job of calling up vivid images in your mind of the events he describes and invites you to empathize with the actual lived experience of the human beings that were there. Really good stuff, but be forewarned: most episodes are better named “audiobooks” and run several hours long. Perfect for long drives, not so great for bite sized enjoyment.

Lore by Aaron Mahnke

My wife turned me on to this one, where Mahnke recounts historically attested tales of weird and creepy stuff. Most of the stories are not (and cannot be) verified as actually happening, but all of them are things that people at least said actually happened - you know, folklore. Even though Mahnke’s cadence includes the occasional hilariously inexplicable pause, he effectively holds your interest and builds tension to creepy climax of each episode. Unlike Hardcore History, these are usually just the right length for listening while doing chores or making a commute.

Writing

How to Do It Yourself

The Elements of Style by William Strunk and E.B. White

This one is the definitive guide to what American authors mean when they say “good writing”. It’s quite brief at a whole 85 pages, and contains advice on everything from comma usage to word choice. Anyone who has ever told you to get rid of unnecessary words, use the active voice, and to prefer the plainest way of saying something possible was speaking in the tradition of Strunk. It’s usefully organized as topical chapters subdivided into brief discussion of pithy principles - as an information architecture nerd, I find that delightful.

On Writing by Stephen King

The classic work by one of the most popular and successful authors of all time, King knows a thing or two about telling stories via the printed word. Like his fiction, the prose is direct, clear, and keeps up a good clip. My one complaint is that it’s structure as a collection of disconnected memories leaves something to be desired in the referencing things later department - but perhaps that’s the point, you have to digest it to get the good stuff out of it

Words that Work by Frank Luntz

A wonderfully pragmatic book that focuses on the importance of how you say it when most of are laser focused on what it is we want to say. While the thrust of the book is on persuasion, as Luntz has worked as a marketer and political speechwriter, the advice to choose your words based on the impact they will have on your audience applies to anything that relies on words. I find it especially useful to compare and contrast with The Elements of Style, which is predominantly focused on aesthetics rather than persuasion.

Nobody Wants to Read Your Sh*t by Steven Pressfield

In this brief work, Pressfield takes the lessons he’s learned writing in different fields - advertising, screenplays, fiction, non-fiction, and self help - and how he applies them across the board. The title reveals the most important lesson: people are busy and don’t want to read anything unless there’s a reason it is compellingly interesting to them - which means you need to develop some serious empathy to get anywhere as a writer. Funny how that keeps popping up about storytelling, right?

Steven Pressfield’s Blog

I’ve referenced Pressfield several times in this series, because I so much enjoy his writing on writing. His blog provides more frequent and bite size doses of what you get in his books. If you aren’t sold on War of Art or Nobody Wants to Read Your Sh*t from my description, pop over to his blog for “Writing Wednesdays” to get a good feel for tone, style, and content.

See Others Do It Well

Invisible Cities by Italo Calvino

This is a much more “literary” book than I usually read and enjoy, but it’s dream-like descriptions of bizarre cities are extremely vivid and compelling. There’s a “twist” at the end that most people know before they come to the book, but honestly, I just enjoyed it for imagining the impossible cities as real fantastic places, and enjoying the near-poetic language used to bring them to life.

Where Good Ideas Come From by Steven Johnson

Most of my examples here of good writing are fiction, because most non-fiction writers must split their effort between getting good at writing and getting good at whatever they’re writing about, whereas fiction authors can focus almost entirely on the craft of making good prose. Steven Johnson’s early works show the signs of that division of labor - Emergence covers absolutely fascinating material and makes extremely valuable connections, but it gets a big clunky to follow at times. His more recent works, however, expertly weave together the disparate facts and insights he shares with you. Honestly, I could have picked from several books, but I like Where Good Ideas Come From the best.

Gates of Fire by Steven Pressfield

Steven Pressfield may not be a Proust or a Hemingway, but I enjoy his books a lot more. Gates of Fire does a great job of describing action in an exciting way while simultaneously exploring the nature of fear, bravery, and loyalty. Required reading at most American military academies and incredibly popular in the special operations community, this is one of the best written books on leadership you can pick up.

Reamde by Neal Stephenson

Again, most folks would not point to Neal Stephenson as a prose stylist, but this book is the perfect example of nailing the execution of a techno-political thriller. As I’ve mentioned before, Stephenson is one of my favorite authors, and I’ve read all of his books, so this one especially stood out by being different in style to his others. The long, information-rich digressions that I so love from Snow Crash and The Baroque Cycle are completely missing here, because this book is all about creating tension and then ratcheting it up relentlessly. Other than Breaking Bad, I have never seen such an expert display of continually increasing stakes: imagine an infinite sequence of falling out of frying pans into fires into frying pans and so on.

Visual Storytelling

How to Do It Yourself

Visual Storytelling and Narrative by Will Eisner

For those of you not familiar with comics, Will Eisner is the guy after whom their biggest award is named - he started writing comics in New York in the 30’s when his high school buddy Bob Kane (creator of Batman) suggested he give it a go, coined the term “graphic novel”, and worked hard to argue and prove that comics could tell serious stories and were a legitimate literary form. This book takes what he learned from his decades of experience creating and teaching sequential art and distills it into a guide to make sure the visuals are always in service to the story - and not the other way around. Beautifully illustrated (duh) and cogently written, this book is useful for anyone who needs to convey messages with a combination of words and images, not just artists and flimmakers.

Understanding Comics by Scott McCloud

This is another meta-work: a comic book about comic books. Why am I harping on comics so much here? Well besides being a big nerd and enjoying them quite a lot, they provide an excellent comparison point to writing (nothing but words) and movies (moving pictures combined with spoken words) that allows examination of what is the same and what is different - and McCloud does exactly that. The central conceptual point of the work is that comic readers are active participants in the creation of the story, because they have to fill in the gaps between every frame. Writers and filmmakers can pull off that trick if they try, but with comics it is absolutely essential and pervasive. That creates a medium with a unique form of engagement worth exploring.

Genndy Tartakovsky - Reading the Action

I found this video when I was looking for examples of composition and pacing done well for the post on Delivery and was immediately hooked. As my previous posts have made no effort to hide, I’m a big fan of everything Genndy Tartakovsky does, and this explained how he does it with remarkable clarity and great examples. I’ve since watched several episodes of Samurai Jack and keep noticing the three techniques identified here. As with comics, I like analyses of animation as a way of learning about film, because the lack of human actors and overt stylization allows you to focus on form and composition and such like very purely.

The Doodle Revolution by Sunni Brown

While this book is not about storytelling exactly, it is about learning to understand and use “visual language”, which can greatly improve communication and understanding, particularly of abstract concepts. This book addresses many of the challenges with learning and using visual language, from people not even stopping to consider it all the way to insecurity over artistic or creative ability hindering visual output. It provides a lot of very concrete guidance on how and when to use visual language in business and personal settings, all without being good at art. Pairs extremely well with Show and Tell above and Gamestorming.

See Others Do It Well

Drive: The Surprising Truth about What Motivates Us by Dan Pink

This quick little video does an excellent job of teaching you something important in a way that is far more memorable and effective than a simple lecture or written chapter. RSA Animate provides the live-drawn whiteboard style graphics to Dan Pink’s narration, and the overall combination of diagrammatic pictures and voice over creates something that will stick with you for years.

Clone Wars Animated Shorts - Directed by Gendy Tartakovsky

I love Samurai Jack, but these shorts combine Tartakovsky’s stylistic elements described in the video linked above with my lifelong love of Star Wars. Taken as a whole, I “joke” that these are my favorite prequel. Originally aired as 3 minute (later expanded to 15 minute) shorts between programs on Cartoon Network, these are incredibly tight little self contained stories that nonetheless build into an overall picture of the Clone War, seamlessly linking the end of Attack of the Clones with the beginning of The Revenge of the Sith. Even if you are not a fan of Star Wars, check out the expert use of environments, composition, and carefully choreographed fight scenes.

300 by Frank Miller

As the only Zack Snyder film I completely enjoy, I give full credit to Frank Miller for the original, which Snyder very directly adapted. Yes, this is the second time Thermopylae has come up in the examples here, but that is to be expected from a classicist infantryman. The graphic novel showcases Miller’s high-contrast style and impressive composition, but also uses size and placement of frames/panels to expertly pull you through the story. It is also just as much a story about storytelling as less gory titles like The Sandman - about the story Western Europe tells itself about its past, the stories a culture tells itself about its values, and the stories we tell ourselves to link our past with our present.

Kwaidan - Directed by Masaki Kobayashi

This movie is visually stunning. It came out in 1964 and I first saw it in 2017, and more than 50 years later, it still looks fantastic. Not “good for its time” but “good for all movies ever made” in a way that is unfortunately rare. It treats us to not one, but four separate expertly told traditional Japanese ghost stories. The Gleeful Iconoclasm of Zhuangzi

Date 2017-July-27

A Note on Spelling Conventions - Skip if you are not a language nerd:

Since westerners started studying Chinese, they have tried to get around the inadequacy of the Latin alphabet to render it. For years the most popular way was the “Wade-Giles System,” which uses various letter combinations as kludges to represent tones. It is a clever way to operate within the constraints of existing letters, but if you don’t know the code, you’re gonna say everything wrong.

More recently, the People’s Republic of China has introduced the pinyin system. The key feature of which is the use of diacritic marks to indicate tones, which is incredibly useful for ignorant westerners like me. With this, you can be much more straightforward in your use of letters, so even if you get the tones wrong, you’re at least getting the rest right. Because of this, I have decided to ditch the Wade-Giles spellings familiar from my youth and use pinyin without the diacritics (because they are a pain in the ass on an English keyboard).

I decided to take advantage of my vacation in China to read some Chinese philosophy in the land that gave it birth. Looking over my bookshelf, I noticed a book I had purchased for a college class - Zhuangzi. In the class we only read a tiny excerpt, and ever since it’s been on my “meaning to read” list. So I ended up pulling it out of my bag and digging in while floating through a landscape that looks exactly like a Chinese landscape painting.

First off, let’s provide a bit of context. “Zhuangzi” can refer to both the philosopher (“Master Zhuang”) or to the work ascribed to him (but probably containing material added later by other people as well). Besides the semi-mythical Laozi and his Daodejing, Zhuangzi is the next most foundational work in Daoism. The philosopher was a rough contemporary of Confucius’s in the 4th century BCE. Both of these eminent gentlemen helped to define the explosion of thinking known as “The Hundred Schools of Thought”. In terms of influence on later philosophy, this time period is roughly analogous to the early Hellenisitic age in the west - a flowering of different approaches that are still discussed, debated, and even followed today. This author of Zhuangzi used this context by telling stories featuring members of other schools of thought - sometimes showing them as fellow seekers of wisdom and other times mercilessly parodying them and their hang ups.

One of the things that struck me in the book was the similarity of the core of the philosophy with Stoicism and Buddhism. Zhuangzi essentially argues that the world is constantly changing and the only way to not be perturbed by this is align yourself with that change, which is an expression of the universal Way (Dao). Despite some important differences, the core idea that we can’t control circumstances, but we can control how we react to them, will sound familiar to students of the two schools I mentioned. I will have to reread the Daodejing, but I don’t remember such a clear parallel there. I suppose I shouldn’t be surprised that such a useful and life-changing mental hack was independently discovered in multiple places.

Which is by no means to say that Zhuangzi is simply a rehash of things you can find elsewhere. It is widely renowned for its imagery, poetry, and imaginative use of metaphor. Some of these struck me as simply bizarre, but many were rather beautiful. One of these poetic sections has become more famous in the west than the book that spawned it: Zhuangzi is the sage who dreamed he was a butterfly and then wondered if he were not a butterfly dreaming of being a man. Besides the imagery, the other ingredient in Zhuangzi’s secret sauce is humor, and there were several times where I would slowly smile or even chuckle as I realized something that I thought was “serious business” was actually the set up for an elaborate joke. When you put these two stylistic traits together, you get a very interesting effect: since you never know exactly where the author stands moment to moment, or even necessarily what he actually means, you have to think for yourself. You can neither buy everything he says uncritically nor dismiss him as someone you disagree with unconditionally. It’s a remarkably effective way to convey something experientially instead of didactically.

To demonstrate this dynamic produced by humor, metaphor, and active engagement, let me share my favorite example. Zhuangzi frequently praises the “useless” and denigrates the “useful.” One image he uses is a gigantic tree that has only been allowed to get so big because no part of it has any usefulness to humans - which would have gotten it cut down or plucked or such like. He similarly points out that useful people get put to work and sometimes even get killed doing that work, and so argues it’s good to be crippled and deformed. I was finding it hard to swallow the whole “useless is good, useful is bad” line of reasoning until one striking metaphor got me:

"A man has to understand the useless before you can talk to him about the useful. The earth is certainly vast and broad, though a man uses no more of it than the area he puts his feet on. If, however, you were to dig away all the earth from around his feet until you reached the Yellow Springs, then would the man still be able to make use of it?”

I had a koan-like flash of insight reading this about all of the other images I had been reading. If not for that moment, I might have written the whole work off as anti-rational nonsense. For you, the “click” might come somewhere else because of a different interaction of the imagery, humor, and your own thoughts.

Though I still find the Daodejing more relevant, Zhuangzi’s skepticism and anti-authoriatarianism was a welcome breath of fresh air in the sometimes puzzling field of Daoism, and gave me a wider understanding of the philosophy than I previously had. It also opened my mind to alternative ways to convey philosophical truths. If you have made previous forays into Chinese philosophy and found them dull or staid, perhaps you should give Zhuangzi a try.

Fragility, Robustness, and Antifragility

Date 2017-June-16

Last time, we discussed a new way of looking at the concept of “risk” and our inability to scientifically define it. Today, we go a little deeper and break systems into three categories based on their exposure to volatility: fragile, robust, and antifragile.

First we need to realize that volatility, time, and risk are really the same thing. This sounds bizarre, right? But let’s return to our least-worst definition of risk: “exposure to negative outcomes brought about by volatility." With this in mind, we can see that given enough time, every possible extreme of volatility will be experienced. To return to our coffee mug example, if you leave that mug on a table in San Francisco, eventually an earthquake will knock it off and break it. So, the combination of its exposure to negative outcomes (falls make it break) plus time (eventually something will happen to knock it off) means that more time equals more likely that it is broken. I should point out, of course, that this concept is statistical and empirical in nature, which has three important implications: 1)it is only really valid probabilistically, 2) it can’t predict the outcome for any one single thing, and 3) it makes no theory-based claims as to why time will eventually break most coffee mugs.

We now know that exposure is the most practical thing to care about, since it definitely matters and we can actually observe it. Contrast this with the other elements of “risk” usually considered - the timing or intensity of the outcomes concerned, which we can’t every really know ahead of time. WIth our focus squarely on exposure, we can now look at how to categorize systems based on exposure, and It turns out that there are three categories: fragile, robust, and antifragile. The first two will be familiar and intuitively obvious, but this last category is novel and thus gives Taleb’s book its name. Let’s go through what each category means and give some examples.

Fragile things are hurt by volatility - eventually something goes wrong and they break. The easy example we’ve been using is the coffee mug. An important concept that we’ve skipped over so far is that these categories are defined by their relationship with volatility, which can be changed by circumstances - it’s not an intrinsic quality, even though some things more inherently tend towards fragility than others.

To understand this distinction, I like to think about potential energy. A brick sitting on top of a ladder has more potential energy than one sitting on the ground. Nothing about the brick changes except its relationship with the gravity well it’s sitting in. Likewise, with our coffee mug, if you wrapped it in bubble wrap, stuffed it in a box, put that box in a waterproof bag, then put that inside a nuke proof bunker filled with packing peanuts, it would be significantly less fragile than sitting on your coffee table, which is less fragile still than balancing on top of a stack of books as you try to open a door.

The most relevant real world examples are traditional stocks and artificial systems that are overly optimized. Sure, traditional stocks go up when volatility goes in their favor, but they go down when it goes down, and you need bigger growth to offset your loss. Highly optimized systems, on the other hand, work great until they catastrophically don’t. These failures are usually due to an unforeseen change in environmental circumstance or an excess of predicted capacity. Traffic is a great example. Highways don’t get linearly slower as cars are added during rush hour - they go from zooming to standstill exponentially quickly as their capacity is exceeded. If you like guns, think about the (somewhat exaggerated) difference between an M16 and an AK-47: an M16 is more accurate and generally higher functioning, but stops working after much less abuse than the less optimized, cruder AK.

So, the AK-47 can be described as “more robust” or “less fragile” than the M16. Robust is usually considered the opposite of fragile, but that’s not quite accurate. Robust things are indifferent to volatility - shit happens and they abide. Volatility neither particularly hurts nor particularly helps them. Think of Notre Dame in Paris - through centuries of wars, invasions, and revolutions, it has remained beautiful and solid and standing. Sure it’s a little rougher around the edges, but it’s not a pile of rubble. Nor is it larger or more beautiful or superior in some way. It simply is.

When it comes to examples of increasing robustness in day to day life, I like to think about my finances and my schedule. As I mentioned before, I keep somewhat large cash savings around, because it makes me more indifferent to volatility. I can pay for the unexpected without indebting myself or scrambling around. Likewise, when scheduling, I build in buffers of time (a trick I originally learned in the army) so that if something unexpected happens (major accident on the freeway, a train stops traffic, there’s a marathon I didn’t know about - whatever) I still get there on time.

Finally, we get to the star of the show, the most novel category: antifragile, the true opposite of fragile. Antifragile systems actually benefit from volatility - eventually something will happen that makes them better or stronger. These systems are most often found in nature. Think of your immune system: once you have chicken pox, you’re immune. Or your muscles: lift weights and you get stronger. Most interesting of all, look at evolution: species and even ecosystems get more efficient and better adapted to their environment over time by responding to the various stressors that come up due to volatility.

Because this is exciting and sounds like a super power, everyone immediately thinks “how can I be more antifragile?” Well, there’s good and bad news. First off, the bad news is that it is more applicable to systems than to individual entities. The good news is that we create systems in our life constantly, and we can engineer those to be as antifragile as possible.

Taleb became wealthy by betting against the market with out of the money put options - when the financial crisis hit and the market tanked, those options paid off. He had no precise knowledge of when a crash would happen or how big it would be, but given enough time it would. So he constructed a system with convex exposure where he accepted finite, limited downside constantly for near unlimited upside in the event of volatility.

For ourselves, it helps to remember that robust is usually a necessary stepping stone to antifragile. Remember the savings we talked about? Step 1 is you don’t care about negative outcomes. Step 2 is to create ways to actively benefit from them - such as by buying undervalued stocks when there is “blood in the streets” or setting up positions that automatically benefit like Taleb’s. Or you might find other ways that disorder in your life can benefit you - have something read or listen to whenever you find yourself unexpectedly delayed, for example. Another favorite of mine is to practice Jocko Willink’s habit of responding to all negative situations with “Good.” Or perhaps, invest in business ideas that take advantage of the seemingly negative, like staffing agencies in a world of declining full time employment.

The basics of Taleb’s framework come pretty easy, but there is so much depth there. For example, I didn’t even touch the math behind it, because that’s not my forte. I promise that as you think about these concepts, you will begin seeing them in your life and trying to find ways to apply them. If you figure out anything interesting, please let me know!

Rethinking Risk with Antifragile

Date: 2017-June-09

Risk fundamentally shapes some of our most critical decisions, but most of us don’t think very deeply or clearly about it. Nassim Taleb is not like most of us. he has thought long, hard, and with great rigor about risk and randomness, and he presents that thinking most usefully and straightforwardly in Antifragile. That being said, while his writing and storytelling are superb, it’s not exactly light beach reading. Below we’re going to talk about just one of the many useful concepts presented in the book, which is a fundamentally different way to think about and evaluate risk.

I’ve been listening to his interview with James Altucher recently, and I was surprised to realize I haven’t written about this book yet. You see, it lives on my shelf of “personal operating manuals” along with Deep Work, Letters from a Stoic, Tools of Titans, and a few others. I am pretty sure that Antifragile is the “80/20” book of his Incerto series - it covers the key concepts, ways to apply them to your life, and just enough of the underlying math to let you know it’s serious (there’s more in the appendix if that gets you going).

Understanding Antifragile requires rethinking the term “risk.” First off, Taleb asserts that there is no such thing as a satisfactory definition of “risk.” Risk as usually defined or described conflates three separate factors - the likelihood of an event, a party’s exposure to that likelihood, and how that party feels about that exposure. Usually you’ll see risk defined as something like “the likelihood of a negative outcome times its severity.”

One problem with this is that “negative outcome” and “severity” can vary enormously depending on the entity experiencing the event. If your coffee cup is knocked off the counter, it’s going to have a bad time. If your cat falls off the counter, it’ll be fine. In both cases the objective event (falling off the counter) is identical, but the entities experiencing it are differently exposed to negative outcomes. When you add in rational thought, you get the further wrinkle that “negative” is a judgment call. While you might think something like “losing money” is intrinsically negative, it’s not so simple. What if I lose money daily but get a huge payout once a year and I’m okay with that? Or what if I decide I’m okay losing the money to have a good time (like a Vegas trip)?

There’s an even thornier challenge besides those above, though: outside of certain fairly constrained bounds, we can’t even accurately model the likelihood of events (we think we can, but we’re very bad at it). Artificial environments that involve chance (like rolling dice or drawing cards) have relatively easy to model probabilities, as do large classes of natural phenomena that fit pretty well into normal distributions. But several classes of events that matter very much to our lives (financial instruments, catastrophic disasters, terrorist attacks, et cetera) cannot have their probability modeled in any meaningful, non-deceiving way.

So where does that leave us for how to think about risk in our own lives? Well, the good news is that while we can’t really nail down a scientific definition of “risk” for the whole wide world, we can do just fine in assessing risk for ourselves. To do so, we need to look at two main factors: how we feel about the outcomes and our exposure to those outcomes taking place. For example, I would very much like to continue living and would find getting killed unambiguously negative. Certain activities, like smoking, riding motorcycles, or going to dangerous neighborhoods dramatically increase my exposure to the outcome of getting killed. I don’t have to have a good model of how likely or unlikely those outcomes are to simply avoid my exposure to that risk.

In terms of personal finance, I keep a lot more cash savings than most investing professionals would advise, because savings help make me more robust - I can be more indifferent to the volatility of the world around me. Air conditioning stops working? Cool, I’ve got cash in the bank. Surprise! Nail in your tire - cash. You get the picture. Now, this works the other way as well - you can take some amount of money that you’re okay with losing and invest it in things with a low chance of paying off but with potentially unlimited returns, like out of the money options or start ups or your own entrepreneurial ventures. Taleb calls this the “barbell method” of investing, because he advocates having heavily safe and heavily risky investments, rather than an averaged portfolio like a business school finance class would teach you to go for.

This may come as a shock to you, but I also find Tim Ferriss’s definition of risk instructive to combine here - he looks at risk by asking whether or not something irreversible and/or massively negative may happen. By using fear setting to mitigate what you can of the likelihood and alter your perception of the negatives, you will often find that very few actions have truly irreversible massive negative outcomes. This attitude can make it easier to expose yourself to the possible upsides of volatility, such as by encouraging you to build your own business or invest a prudent amount in a highly speculative company.

So how do you apply these concepts? We’ve talked about a few in our examples above, but here’s my top pics for most useful ways to re-think your approach to risk in your life:

Career: Corporate jobs are generally viewed as “safe” because there is very little volatility in your paycheck. Until it’s totally gone. Maybe you get laid off, or maybe you happened to be working for Enron. If this happens when you’re 23, it’s not such a big deal, but if it happens right before retirement, it can be devestating. Compare this with a skilled individual worker or independent consultant, and their pay is much more variable day to day and month to month, but they have a much tighter feedback loop: as long as you keep getting clients, you’re doing something valuable. When you start losing your ability to find them, it’s a sign you need to move on and find a new gig.

Investing: Before we get into this, please bear in mind I am not an investment advisor of any sort, and this does not constitute specific financial advice - this paragraph is just a recommendation on where to look to learn more, not specific investment strategies. As mentioned above, a traditionally balanced portfolio basically says “let’s get rid of all the volatility except what’s inherent in the market.” The trouble is, there’s a whole damn lot of volatility inherent in the market, especially when you are looking only at equity. Much better to figure out however much money you need to not be ruined and protect it from volatility the best you can (Inflation-protected Treasuries or the like) and take whatever is left over and put it into things where you have exposure to the potential for massive upside - investing in your friend’s genius start up, opening a restaurant, writing a novel, out of the money options, whatever.

Health and Behavior: There are some things that can greatly affect our exposure to negative health outcomes - like the previously mentioned smoking or engaging in dangerous stunts. Many of these you can completely opt out of and face no risk - which is the approach recommended by Taleb. You’ll have to decide for yourself if a non-zero chance of horrible death is worth the benefits of doing things like motorcycle riding or sky diving. On the more positive side, doing things like lifting heavy weights and walking frequently have close to zero downside (some chance of injury with weight lifting), but potentially tremendous upside to your health long term.

This is just the tip of the iceberg with Taleb’s material, and I strongly recommend you check out not only Antifragile, but the rest of the Incerto series. We haven’t even gotten into just what “Antifragility” is, but I wanted to cover the basic approach to risk and provide some tangible ways to use it first.
